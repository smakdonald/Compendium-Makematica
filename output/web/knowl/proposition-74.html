Proposition 2.27.

Assume \(R\) is a ring, \(W\) an \(R\)-module, and \(i: V \to W\) an injective \(R\)-module homomorphism. We say \(i\) is a {} if there exists an \(R\)-module homomorphism \(q: W \to V\) such that \(q \circ i = id_V\) (i.e., \(q(i(v)) = v\) for all \(v \in V\)).
  2. Prove that if \(R\) is a field, \(V\) is a subspace (i.e., submodule) of \(W\text{,}\) and \(i\) is the inclusion map, then \(i\) is a split injection. Note that since \(i\) is the inclusion map, what you need to prove is that there exists an \(R\)-module homomorphism \(q: W \to V\) such that \(q(v) = v\text{.}\) {}: Start by picking a basis \(B\) of \(V\) and use a theorem proven in class to show that \(B\) can be extended to a basis \(C\) of \(W\text{.}\) Use \(C\) to construct \(q\text{.}\) (I am assuming that \(V\) is a subspace of \(W\) and that \(i\) is the inclusion map just for simplicity â€” more generally, it is true that every injective \(R\)-module homomorphism is a split injection whenever \(R\) is a field.)
  3. Assume \(R\) is a non-zero integral domain, but that it is not a field. Prove there exists an \(R\)-module homomorphism that is an injection but {} a split injection. {}: Pick \(a \in R\) such that \(a \neq 0\) and \(a\) is not a unit, let \(I\) be the proper ideal \(I = R \cdot a\) generated by \(a\) and show the inclusion map \(i: I \to R\) is not a split injection.
