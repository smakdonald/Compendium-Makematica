  1. Notice \(\cp_A(x)=\det(A-xI)\text{.}\) So we have
    \begin{equation*} \begin{align}\cp_A(x)&=-x\det\begin{bmatrix} -x & 0 \\ 1 & -2-x\end{bmatrix}-(-1)\det\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -2-x\end{bmatrix}+2\det\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -x \\ 0 & 1\end{bmatrix}\\&=-x(-x(-2-x))+(-2-x)+2(1)\\&=-x^3-2x^2-x\\&-x(x^2+2x+1)\\&=-x(x+1)^2.\end{align} \end{equation*}
    Not so bad! Now, the invariant factors all divide the characteristic polynomial and must divide the following factor, so our options for sets of invariant factors are the following: - \(\{-x(x+1)^2\}\) - \(\{(x+1),-x(x+1)\}\) However, the largest invariant factor is also the minimal polynomial. So we check to see if \(-A(A+I)=0\text{.}\) Luckily, the very first calculation shows that this is not the case. Thus the minimum polynomial is the characteristic polynomial is the only invariant factor of \(A\text{.}\) Thus the RCF of \(A\) is
    \begin{equation*} C(-x^3-2x^2-x)=\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \\0 & 1 & 2\end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
  2. Luckily for us, \(\cp_A(x)\) factors completely into linear terms! So our elementary divisors are \(-x\) and \((x+1)^2\text{.}\) We see \(J_1(0)=\begin{bmatrix} 0 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(J_2(-1)=\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 1 & -1\end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) so the Jordan Canonical form of \(A\) is
    \begin{equation*} J=\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \\0 & 1 & -1\end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
