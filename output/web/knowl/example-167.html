Example B.5.

An important, general observation is that, any group \(G\) and \(N \unlhd G\text{,}\) since \(gNg^{-1} = N\) for all \(g\text{,}\) it follows that \(N\) is necessarily a union of conjugacy classes. In other words, the action of \(G\) on itself by conjugation restricts to an action on \(N\) since \(N\) is normal, and thus \(N\) is a union of orbits of this action. Moreover, if \(G\) is finite then, by Lagrange, \(|N| \mid |G|\text{.}\) Finally, \(N\) certainly contains \(e\text{.}\) Putting these facts together we get than \(|N|\) must both divide \(|G|\) and be a sum of cardinalities of conjugacy classes, including the class \(\{e\}\text{.}\)
For example, if \(N \unlhd S_4\text{,}\) then \(|N| \mid 24\) and \(|N|\) must equal \(1\) plus the sum of some sub-list of \(6, 8, 6, 3\text{.}\) The only possibilities are
\begin{equation*} 1, 1 + 3, 1 + 3 + 8, \and 1 + 6 + 8 + 6 + 3 \end{equation*}
Having determined the sizes, also determines what the corresponding set \(N\) is. Let me stress that just because the size of \(N\) satisfies the restrictions given by Lagrangeâ€™s theorem and Corollary [provisional cross-reference: lemnormconj] it does not automatically follow that \(N\) really is a subgroup or that it is normal. (In other words these are just necessary conditions.) So we still need to check whether the candidate sets we found do indeed give normal subgroups.
The first and last case represent the boring normal subgroups: \(\{e\}\) and \(G\text{.}\) The case \(1 + 3\) also represents a normal subgroup, which consists of all the products of all product of two disjoint two cycles and the identity:
\begin{equation*} V=\{e, (1 2)(3 4), (1 3)(2 4), (1 4)(2 3)\}. \end{equation*}
The last case also corresponds to a normal subgroup which is \(A_4\text{.}\)
