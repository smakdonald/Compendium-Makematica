Proof

Let \(V = F^n\) and \(W = F^m\) and \(g: V \to W\) be the linear transformation given by \(g(v) = A \cdot v\text{,}\) matrix multiplication. (I have called this map \(T_A\) in the past.) If \(B'\) and \(C'\) are the standard basis of \(V\) and \(W\) then \([g]_{B'}^{C'} = A\text{.}\)
The Theorem gives that there are bases \(B\) and \(C\) of \(V\) and \(W\) such that
\begin{equation*} [g]_B^C = \begin{bmatrix} I_r & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \\ \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
So, by Proposition , we have
\begin{equation*} A = [g]_{B'}^{C'} = [\id_W]_C^{C'}[g]_B^C [\id_V]_{B'}^{B}=P \begin{bmatrix}I_r & 0 \\0 & 0 \\\end{bmatrix}Q \end{equation*}
with \(P := [\id_W]_C^{C'}\) and \(Q := [\id_V]_{B'}^{B}\text{.}\) Note that \(P, Q\) are invertible since they are change of basis matrices (see ).
The uniqueness of \(r\) follows from the fact that \(r = \rank_F(g)\text{,}\) which does not depend on a choice of bases.
