Proof

Consider the polynomial \(x^2-3\text{.}\) This has a root \(\sqrt{3}\text{,}\) which is not in \(\Q\text{.}\) Thus, as \(x^2-3\) has degree 2, it is irreducible by Theorem 2.2 [provisional cross-reference: empty], making it the minimal polynomial of \(\Q(\sqrt{3})\text{.}\) Thus by Theorem 2.31 [provisional cross-reference: empty] we see that \([\Q(\sqrt{3}):\Q]=2\text{.}\)
Next, consider the polynomial \(x^2+1\text{.}\) This has a root \(i\text{,}\) which is not in \(\Q(\sqrt[3]{2})\) as \(\Q(\sqrt[3]{2})\subseteq\R\) and \(i\not\in\R\text{.}\) Let \(K=\Q(\sqrt[3]{2})\) for convenience. As \(x^2+1\text{,}\) has degree 2, it is irreducible by Theorem 2.2 [provisional cross-reference: empty], making it the minimal polynomial of \(K(i)\text{.}\) Thus by Theorem 2.31 [provisional cross-reference: empty] we see that \([K(i):K]=2\text{.}\) Thus by the degree formula [provisional cross-reference: empty] we have \([K(i):\Q]=[K(i):K][K:\Q]=2(2)=4\text{.}\)
By Example 2.22 [provisional cross-reference: empty] we see that \([\Q[\sqrt[3]{2}]:\Q]=3\text{.}\) However, as \(\sqrt[3]{2}\) is the root of \(x^3-2\) it is algebraic over we see that \(\Q[\sqrt[3]{2}]=\Q(\sqrt[3]{2})\) and thus that \([\Q(\sqrt[3]{2}):\Q]=3\text{.}\)
Next, consider the polynomial \(x^2+1\text{.}\) This has a root \(i\text{,}\) which is not in \(\Q(\sqrt[3]{2})\) as \(\Q(\sqrt[3]{2})\subseteq\R\) and \(i\not\in\R\text{.}\) Let \(L=\Q(\sqrt[3]{2})\) for convenience. As \(x^2+1\text{,}\) has degree 2, it is irreducible by Theorem 2.2 [provisional cross-reference: empty], making it the minimal polynomial of \(L(i)\text{.}\) Thus by Theorem 2.31 [provisional cross-reference: empty] we see that \([L(i):L]=2\text{.}\) Thus by the degree formula [provisional cross-reference: empty] we have \([L(i):\Q]=[L(i):L][L:\Q]=2(3)=6\text{.}\)
Consider \(\gamma=\sqrt{3}+2i\text{,}\) and note that \(\gamma^2=-1+4\sqrt{3}i\) and \(\gamma^3=-9\sqrt{3}+10i\text{.}\) Thus \(\frac{1}{28}(\gamma^3+9\gamma)=i\) and \(\frac{-1}{14}(\gamma^3+9\gamma)=\sqrt{3}\text{.}\) Thus \(\gamma\) is a primitive element of this extension field.
Next, consider the monic polynomial \(x^4+2x^2+49\text{,}\) and observe
\begin{equation*} \begin{align} 0&=x^4+2x^2+49\\ -48&=x^4+2x^2+1\\ (16)(3)(-1)&=(x^2+1)^2\\ 4\sqrt{3}i&=(x^2+1)\\ -1+4\sqrt{3}i&=x^2. \end{align} \end{equation*}
However, as \(\gamma^2=-1+4\sqrt{3}i\text{,}\) we see that \(\gamma\) is the root of this polynomial. Since this is a monic polynomial of degree \(4=[K(i):\Q]\text{,}\) it must be the minimal polynomial as well. Thus the minimal polynomial of \([K(i):\Q]=x^4+2x^2+49\text{.}\)
Consider \(\gamma=\sqrt[3]{2}i\text{,}\) and note that \(\frac{-1}{2}\gamma^{-2}=\frac{-1}{2}\frac{1}{-\sqrt[3]{2}^2}=\frac{-1}{2}\frac{\sqrt[3]{2}}{-2}=\sqrt[3]{2}\text{,}\) and \(2\gamma^{-3}=2\frac{-1}{2i}=2\frac{i}{2}=i\text{.}\)
Consider \(x^6-4\text{,}\) and note that \(\gamma^2=2^{2/3}\text{,}\) and thus \(\gamma^6=2^2=4\text{.}\) Thus \(\gamma^6-4=0\text{,}\) so \(\gamma\) is a root of this polynomial. Since this is a monic polynomial of degree \(6=[L(i):\Q]\text{,}\) it must be the minimal polynomial as well. Thus the minimal polynomial of \([L(i):\Q]=x^6-4\text{.}\)
