Example 3.25. Abelianization.

Let \(G\) be any group. For \(x,y \in G\text{,}\) set \([x,y] = xyx^{-1}y^{-1}\text{.}\) Let \(G'\) denote the commutator subgroup of \(G\) generated by all elements of the form \([x,y]\) for \(x,y \in G\text{.}\) (Some people write \(G'\) as \([G,G]\text{.}\)) Then \(G'\) is in fact normal: \(G' \unlhd G\text{.}\) Now let \(f: G \to A\) be any group homomorphism from \(G\) to an abelian group \(A\text{.}\) Since \(f([x,y]) = [f(x),f(y)] = e\) for all \(x, y \in G\) (since \(A\) is abelian), we have that \(\operatorname{Ker}(f)\) must contain \(G'\text{.}\) By the UMP for quotients, we get that \(f\) factors as
\begin{equation*} f:G \xrightarrow{{\pi}} G/G' \xrightarrow{\overline{f}} A \end{equation*}
for a unique group homomorphism \(\overline{f}\text{.}\)
The group \(G/G'\) is called the abelianization of \(G\) and the motto is: A homomorphism from a group to an abelian group factors uniquely through the abelianization.
