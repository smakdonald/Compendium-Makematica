Furthermore, since \(H\cap K\) is a subgroup of both \(H\) and \(K\) we have by Lagrange's theorem that \(|H\cap K|\mid \operatorname{gcd}(q,p)=1\text{,}\) so that \(H\cap K=\{e_G\}\text{.}\) From here it follows that
\begin{equation*} |HK|=\frac{|H||K|}{|H\cap K|}=\frac{q\cdot p}{1}=pq=|G| \end{equation*}
and so \(HK=G\text{.}\) The recognition theorem now yields that
\begin{equation*} G \cong H \rtimes_\rho K \cong C_q\rtimes_\rho C_p. \end{equation*}
for some homomorphism \(\rho: K \to \operatorname{Aut}(H)\text{,}\) equivalently \(\rho: C_p \to \operatorname{Aut}(C_q)\text{.}\) By the UNM of cyclic groups to give such a homomorphism \(\rho\) is equivalent to giving an element \(z\in \operatorname{Aut}(C_q)\) so that \(z^p=\text{id}\text{,}\) which will give \(\rho(y^j)=z^j\) for \(C_p=\langle y\rangle\text{.}\) Thus \(|z|\mid p\) yielding that either \(|z|=1\) or \(|z|=p\text{.}\)
