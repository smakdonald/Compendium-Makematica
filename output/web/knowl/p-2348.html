Let \(T:V \to V\) be the linear operator given by \(T(p) = xp'\) (where \(p'\) denotes the derivative of \(p).\)
  1. Note that the change of basis matrix for this operator is given by
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & n-1 \\ \end{bmatrix}, \end{equation*}
    with the basis \(\{1,x,\dots,x^{n-1}\}\text{.}\) Thus \(\cp_A(x)\) will be given by the determinant of the matrix
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} x & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & x-1 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & x-(n-1) \\ \end{bmatrix}, \end{equation*}
    which is diagonal. Hence \(\cp_AT(x)=\displaystyle\prod_{i=0}^{n-1}(x-i)\text{.}\)
  2. Our \(\cp_AT(x)\) factors into distinct linear polynomials, each of which is in the form \((x-i)^1\) for \(0\leq i\leq n-1\text{.}\) Thus each linear term is an elementary divisor. However, as none of these elementary divisors divide any of the others, we see that the only invariant factor is \(\cp_AT(x)\) itself.
  3. As each linear term is an elementary divisor, each Jordan block a \(1\times1\) matrix with \(i\) as the only entry. Thus the Jordan Canonical form is
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & n-1 \\ \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    As the only invariant factor is \(\cp_AT(x)\text{,}\) we see that the Rational Canonical Form of \(T\) is \(C(\cp_AT(x))\text{.}\)
