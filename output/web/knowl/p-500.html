Define a map \(f: F \setminus \{0\}\to G/H\) as the composition of
\begin{equation*} F \setminus \{0\} \xrightarrow{g} \operatorname{GL}_n(F) \twoheadrightarrow\operatorname{GL}_n(F)/\operatorname{SL}_n(F) \end{equation*}
where \(g(x)\) is the \(n \times n\) matrix with \(x\) in the upper-left corner, \(1\)'s along the rest of the diaganal, and \(0\)'s everywhere else, and the second map is the canonical surjection. Then \(g\) is easily seen to be a homomorphism and hence so is \(f\) (since the composition of homomorhisms is a homomorphism). I claim \(f\) is one-to-one and onto. For any \(A \in \operatorname{GL}_n(F)\text{,}\) let \(x = \det(A)\) and note that \(g(x)^{-1} \cdot A\) has determinant \(1\) and thus belongs to \(\operatorname{SL}_n(F)\text{.}\) So \(f(x) = g(x)\operatorname{SL}_n(F) = A \operatorname{SL}_n(F)\text{.}\) This proves \(f\) is onto. If \(f(x) = e\text{,}\) then \(g(x) \operatorname{SL}_n(F) = \operatorname{SL}_n(F)\) and so \(g(x) \in \operatorname{SL}_n(F)\text{.}\) Clearly this means \(x = 1\text{.}\)
