\(\thm\) - FTFGMOPIDEDF.
Let \(R\) be a PID and let \(M\) be a finitely generated \(R\)-module. Then there exist integers \(r \geq 0, k \geq 0\text{,}\) prime elements \(p_1,\ldots,p_s\) of \(R\) (not necessarily distinct), and integers \(e_1,\ldots ,e_s \geq 1\text{,}\) such that
\begin{equation*} M \cong R^r \oplus R/(p_1^{e_1}) \oplus \cdots \oplus R/(p_s^{e_s}). \end{equation*}
Moreover \(r\) and \(k\) are uniquely determined by \(M\text{,}\) and the list \(p_1^{e_1},\ldots, p_s^{e_s}\) is unique up to associates and reordering.
Proof..
First write \(M\) in invariant factor form \(M \cong R^r \oplus R/(d_1) \oplus \dots \oplus R/(d_k)\text{.}\) For any non-zero, non-unit element \(d\) of \(R\text{,}\) we have \(d = p_1^{e_1} \cdots p_m^{e_m}\) for some distinct (non-associate) prime elements \(p_j\) and integers \(e_j \geq 1\text{.}\) By the Chinese remainder theorem (applied over and over again) we have
\begin{equation*} R/(d)\cong R/(p_1^{e_1}) \oplus \cdots \oplus R/(p_m^{e_m}) \end{equation*}
Doing this for each fact \(R/(d_i)\) in the invariant factor form of \(M\) we obtain the existence of an elementary divisor form of \(M\text{.}\)
Uniqueness follows from the uniqueness of the invariant factor form and of the prime factorizations of the \(d_i\)'s.
