  • Existence of Composition Series for Finite Groups.
    Assume \(G\) is a finite group. If \(G = \{e\}\) then taking \(k = 0\) in the definition gives a composition series of \(G\text{.}\) If \(G\) is simple, then taking \(k=1\) does.
    Otherwise, thre is a non-trivial and proper normal subgroup \(N\) of \(G\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} e \lhd N \lhd G. \end{equation*}
    By induction on \(\# G\text{,}\) both \(N\) and \(G/N\) have composition series, and so by the Proposition, so does \(G\text{.}\)
  • Uniquesness of Composition Series up to Equivalence.
    Suppose we are given two composition series
    \begin{equation*} \{e\} = N_0 \unlhd N_1 \unlhd N_2 \unlhd\cdots \unlhd N_k = G \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \{e\} = M_0 \unlhd N_1 \unlhd N_2 \unlhd\cdots \unlhd M_j = G \end{equation*}
    for the same group \(G\text{.}\) By the previous Theorem, they admit equivalent refinements. But, by the 4-th Isomorphism Theorem, since \(N_i/N_{i-1}\) is simple, there are no normal subgroups of \(N_i\) that contain \(N_{i-1}\) besides \(N_i\) and \(N_{i-1}\text{.}\) So, the only refinements of a composition series are those obtained by including repetitions of the subgroups. This shows that the list of factors in these refinements are the original factors along with copies of the trivial group. Since the refinements are equivalent, the number of non-trivial factors in each is the same, and these non-trivial factors are precisely the factors occuring in the two original normal series.
