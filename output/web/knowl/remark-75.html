Remark 13.54.

For a ring \(R\text{,}\) recall that \(\GL_n(R)\) is the group of invertible \(n \times n\) matrices with entries in \(R\text{.}\) Let \(E_n(R)\) be the subset of \(\GL_n(R)\) consisting of all products of elementary matrices of type I. Since the inverse of an elementary matrix of type I is again an elementary matrix of type I, \(E_n(R)\) is a subgroup of \(\GL_n(R)\text{.}\) In fact it is a normal subgroup, at least when \(n \geq 3\text{,}\) and hence the quotient group \(\GL_n(R)/E_n(R)\) is defined in this case.
As a consequence of the Theorem above, if \(R = F\) is a field then there is an isomorphism of groups \(\GL_n(F)/E_n(F) \cong F^\times\) where \(F^\times = (F \setminus \{0\}, \cdot)\) is the group of units in \(F\text{.}\)
For a general ring, the extent to which \(\GL_n(R)/E_n(R)\) is â€œlarger'' than \(R^\times\) measure the extent to which Theorem fails.
The algebraic \(K\)-group \(K_1(R)\) is defined to be \(\GL_\infty(R)/E_\infty(R)\) (and it is usually isomorphic to \(\GL_n(R)/E_n(R)\) for \(n \gg 0\)). Moreover, \(E_\infty(R)\) is the derived subgroup of \(\GL_\infty(R)\text{,}\) and hence \(K_1(R)\) is the abelianization of \(\GL_\infty(R)\text{.}\)
