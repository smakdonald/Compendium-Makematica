Let \(i: N \to M\) be any injection of left \(K[G]\)-modules. It suffices to prove that there is an \(K[G]\)-linear map \(p: M \to N\) such that \(p \circ i = \mathrm{id}_N\text{.}\) By restriction of scalars along the inclusion \(K \subseteq K[G]\text{,}\) we may regard \(i\) as a \(K\)-linear map between \(K\)-vector spaces. As such it admits a \(K\)-linear splitting \(f: M \twoheadrightarrow N\) (since \(K\) is semisimple). There is no reason that \(f\) will be \(K[G]\)-linear, but we can modify it so that it becomes so: Define \(p: M \to N\) by
\begin{equation*} p(m) = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} g^{-1} f(g m). \end{equation*}
Note that the formula makes sense since \(|G|\) is invertible in \(K\) by assumption.
Then \(p\) is still a \(K\)-linear map (since \(f\) is \(K\)-linear and the group action is \(K\)-linear). For any \(h \in G\) we have
\begin{equation*} p(hm) = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} g^{-1} f(g hm) = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{x \in G} hx^{-1} f(xm) = h p(m), \end{equation*}
where the second equality is given by identifying \(x\) with \(gh\text{.}\) These conditions ensure that \(p\) is \(K[G]\)-linear. Finally,
\begin{equation*} p(i(n))= \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} g^{-1} f(g i(n)) = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} g^{-1} f(i(gn)) = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} g^{-1} gn = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} n = n \end{equation*}
where the second equality uses that \(i\) is \(K[G]\)-linear and the third one uses that \(f \circ i = \mathrm{id}\text{.}\)
