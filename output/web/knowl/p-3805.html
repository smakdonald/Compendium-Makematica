  1. Notice that \(J\) is a triangular matrix with \(0\)'s along the diagonal. Thus \(\cp_J(x)=x^{2n}\text{,}\) the product of the diagonal entries of the matrix \(J-xI\text{.}\)
    Squaring a triangular matrix moves everything one down (Proof?), and so \(x^{2n-1}\) should do it. Squaring \(J\) just moves us one closer, so \(x^{2n-2}\)
  2. As \(\cp_{J^2}(x)=x^{2n}\text{,}\) the only roots of it are \(0\text{,}\) and thus these are the only elementary divisors of \(J^2\text{.}\)
