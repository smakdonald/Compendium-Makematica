Proof

Let \(\Q(i) = \{a + bi \mid a, b \in \Q\}\) and \(\Z[i] = \{a + ib \mid a, b \in \Z\}\text{.}\)
Suppose by way of contradiction that \(7\) is not prime in \(\Z[i]\text{.}\) Thus \(7=\alpha\beta\text{,}\) with \(\alpha,\beta\) non-units and where \(\alpha=a+bi\) and \(\beta=c+di\text{,}\) for \(a,b,c,d\in Z\text{.}\) Thus \(\overline{7}=\overline{\alpha\beta}=\overline{\alpha}\overline{\beta}\text{.}\) As \(\overline{7+0i}=49\text{,}\) we see that \(\overline{\alpha}=(a^2+b^2)|49\text{.}\) Note that the possible factorizations of 49 in \(\Z\) are \((1)(49)\) and \((7)(7)\text{.}\)
However, if \(\overline{\alpha}=49\) then \(\overline{\beta}=1\text{,}\) making it a unit. So \(\overline{\alpha}=7\) and thus \(a^2+b^2=7\text{.}\) As \(a^2, b^2\leq0\text{,}\) either \(a=0\) or \(b=0\text{.}\) But then either \(a^2=7\) or \(b^2=7\text{,}\) and as \(\sqrt{7}\not\in\Z\text{,}\) we have a contradiction. Thus \(7\) is prime in \(\Z[i]\text{.}\) Using Eisenstein’s Criterion with \(p=7\text{,}\) we see that \(x^6 + 7\) is irreducible in \(\Q(i)[x]\text{.}\)
Using Eisenstein’s Criterion with \(p=2\text{,}\) we see that \(x^4 - 6x^2 + 10\) is irreducible in \(\Q[x]\text{.}\) However, in \(\Q(i)[x]\) observe that
\begin{equation*} x^4-6x^2+10=(x^2-(3+i))(x^2-(3-i)). \end{equation*}
As neither \((x^2-(3+i))\) nor \((x^2-(3-i))\) are units in \(\Q(i)[x]\) (as \(x\) has no inverse), we see that \(x^4-6x^2+10\) is reducible in \(\Q(i)[x]\text{.}\)
