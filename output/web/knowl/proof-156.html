Proof

Since \(R\) is left semisimple, we have \(R \cong I_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus I_t\) with each \(I_i\) is simple (in fact a minimal ideal). Group by isomorphism to rewrite this as \(R \cong M_1^{\oplus n_1} \oplus \cdots \oplus M_m^{\oplus n_m}\) with each \(M_i\) simple, \(n_j \geq 1\text{,}\) and such that \(M_i\) is not isomorphic to \(M_j\) for all \(i \ne j\text{.}\) We compute the endomorphisms of both sides:
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \label{E912} \operatorname{End}_R(R) &=& \mathrm{Hom}_R(\bigoplus_{i=1}^m M_i^{\oplus n_i}, \prod_{j=1}^m M_j^{\oplus n_j}) \cong \prod_j \mathrm{Hom}_R(M_i^{\oplus n_i}, \prod_i M_i^{\oplus n_j})\\ &\cong& \prod_{i=1}^m \prod_{j=1}^m \mathrm{Hom}_R(M_i^{\oplus n_i}, M_j^{\oplus n_j}) \\ &=& \prod_{i=1}^m \mathrm{Hom}_R(M_i^{\oplus n_i}, M_i^{\oplus n_i}) \\ &=& \prod_{i=1}^m \operatorname{End}_R(M_i^{\oplus n_i})\cong \prod_{i=1}^m \operatorname{Mat}_{n_i}(\operatorname{End}_R(M_i)). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
Above the second line follows from the first by properties of \(\mathrm{Hom}\text{,}\) the third follows because Schur’s lemma gives that \(\mathrm{Hom}_R(M_i,M_j)=0\text{,}\) and consequently \(\mathrm{Hom}_R(M_i^{\oplus n_i},M_j^{\oplus n_j})=0\text{,}\) when \(i\neq j\text{.}\) The final isomorphism is the previous lemma.
On the one hand, we have \(\operatorname{End}_R(R) \cong R^{\mathrm{op}}\) by a problem from the homework. On the other hand, applying Schur’s Lemma again, \(D'_i := \operatorname{End}_R(M_i)\) is a division ring for all \(i\text{.}\)
Combining these gives
\begin{equation*} R^{\mathrm{op}} \cong \operatorname{Mat}_{n_1}(D'_1) \times \cdots \times \operatorname{Mat}_{n_m}(D'_m) \end{equation*}
and hence, also by a homework problem, we have
\begin{equation*} R \cong \left(\operatorname{Mat}_{n_1}(D'_1) \times \cdots \times \operatorname{Mat}_{n_m}(D'_m)\right)^{\mathrm{op}} \cong \operatorname{Mat}_{n_1}(D_1) \times \cdots \times \operatorname{Mat}_{n_m}(D_m) \end{equation*}
with \(D_i := (D_i')^{\mathrm{op}}=\operatorname{End}_R(M_i)^\mathrm{op}\text{.}\)
This shows that given a decomposition of \(R\) as a left semisimple module, there is a ring decomposition as a product of matrix rings over division rings, and the data of division rings and matrix sizes is related to the data of simple modules and multiplicities by the formulas (1)–(3). We just need to prove uniqueness.
Say we are given an isomorphism of rings \(R \cong \prod_{i=1}^k \operatorname{Mat}_{t_i}(Q_i)\) for some division rings \(Q_1,\dots,Q_k\text{.}\) Then since \(\operatorname{Mat}_{t_i}(Q_i)\) decomposes as a direct sum of \(t_i\) copies of \(N_i:=Q_i^{t_i}\text{,}\) and \(N_i\) is a simple \(\operatorname{Mat}_{t_i}(Q_i)\)-module, hence also a simple \(R\)-module, we have a semisimple \(R\)-module decomposition of \(R\) as
\begin{equation*} M_1^{\oplus n_1} \oplus \cdots \oplus M_m^{\oplus n_m} \cong R \cong N_1^{\oplus t_1} \oplus \cdots \oplus N_k^{\oplus t_k}. \end{equation*}
By Krull-Schmidt, we must have \(m=k\text{,}\) and after a permutation, \(M_i \cong N_i = Q_i^{\oplus t_i}\) and \(n_i=t_i\) for each \(i\text{.}\)
Moreover, we have
\begin{equation*} D_i\cong \operatorname{End}_R(M_i)^{\mathrm{op}} \cong \operatorname{End}_R(N_i)^{\mathrm{op}}. \end{equation*}
We recall that \(N_i\cong Q_i^{\oplus n_i}\text{,}\) with the natural column vector action from \(\operatorname{Mat}_{n_i}(Q_i)\text{,}\) and the trivial action from the other factors. Thus,
\begin{equation*} \operatorname{End}_R(N_i)^{\mathrm{op}} = \operatorname{End}_{\operatorname{Mat}_{n_i}(Q_i)} (Q_i^{\oplus n_i}) ^{\mathrm{op}}\cong Q_i, \end{equation*}
using the exercise above.
