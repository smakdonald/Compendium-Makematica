Let \(D\) be a finite integral domain.
  1. Let \(x\in D\text{,}\) and consider the set \(\{x^n|n\in\N\}\text{.}\) As \(D\) is finite there must exist \(n<m\in\N\) such that \(x^n=x^m\text{.}\) In integral domains cancellation is allowed, and thus we can cancel an \(x^n\) from both sides, yielding \(e=x^{m-n}\text{,}\) and thus \(x\cdot x^{m-n-1}=e\text{,}\) making \(x\) a unit of \(D\text{.}\) Thus \(D\) is a field.
  2. Let \(R\) be a commutative ring and \(P\subseteq R\) a prime ideal such that \(P\) has finite index as an additive subgroup of \(R\text{.}\)
    Consider \(R/P\text{,}\) which is a domain since \(P\) is a prime ideal. As \([R:P]=n\) for some \(n\in\N\text{,}\) we know that \(|R/P|=n\text{,}\) making it a finite domain. Thus \(R/P\) is a field by Part (a), making \(P\) maximal by the same theorem as above.
    Let \(R=\Z\) and consider \(R[x]\text{,}\) in which \((x)\) is a prime ideal. However, \(R[x]/(x)=\Z\text{,}\) which does not have finite order, nor is it a field.
