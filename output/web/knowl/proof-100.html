Proof

Let \(F\subseteq L\) be fields and let \(x, y \in L\) be algebraic elements over \(F\) First, notice that \(xy\) and \(x+y\) are contained in \(F(x,y)\text{.}\) As \(y\) is algebraic over \(F\text{,}\) it is the root of some polynomial \(f\) with coefficients in \(f\text{.}\) But \(f\) is also contained in \(F(x)\text{,}\) and thus the extension \([F(x,y):F(x)]\) is algebraic. As \([F(x):F]\) is algebraic as well, we see that \([F(x,y):F]\) is an algebraic extension of fields. Thus \(xy\) and \(x+y\) are algebraic over \(F\text{.}\) #### \(\rem\) The converse of this proposition is also true: Given field extensions \(F \subseteq L \subseteq E\text{,}\) if \(F \subseteq E\) is algebraic then so are \(L \subseteq E\) and \(F \subseteq L\text{.}\) This is more or less obvious from the definition.
