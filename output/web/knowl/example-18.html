Example 28.11.

Let \(G=C_3\text{.}\) We can use Maschkeâ€™s Theorem and the theory of semisimple rings so far to classify every representation of \(G\) over \({\mathbb{R}}\) or over \(\mathbb{C}\) (or more generally over any field of characteristic not equal to 3). In any case, the left regular representation \(V\) of \(C_3\) is the three-dimensional representation with basis \(\{1,g,g^2\}\) such that \(g\cdot 1 = g, g\cdot g=g^2, g\cdot g^2 =1\text{,}\) i.e.,
\begin{equation*} g \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
in this basis. Also in any case, the subspace \(W\) spanned by \(1+g+g^2\text{,}\) which is the vector \((1,1,1)\) in these coordinates, is a 1-dimensional \(G\)-stable subspace, so a simple subrepresentation. Moreover, this is the trivial representation, since this vector is fixed by \(g\text{.}\) Then \(V/W\) obtains the structure of a representation. We can take \(1+W,g+W\) as a basis for \(V/W\text{,}\) and \(g\cdot (1+W) = g+W\text{,}\) and \(g\cdot (g+W) = g^2+W = -1-g +W\text{,}\) i.e., in our coordinates,
\begin{equation*} g\mapsto \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
A \(G\)-stable subspace must correspond to an eigenvector of \(g\) (which is equivalently an eigenvector of \(g^{-1}\)). The characteristic equation of this matrix shows that the eigenvalues are precisely the primitive cube roots of unity.
If \(K={\mathbb{R}}\) (or more generally if there are no primitive cube roots of unity), then the \(2\)-dimensional representation \(V/W\) we just found is simple since there are no stable subspaces. If \(K=\mathbb{C}\) (or more generally if there are primitive cube roots of unity), let \(\omega\) be a primitive cube root of unity. The \(2\)-dimensional representation \(V/W\) has \(\omega\) and \(\omega^2\) as eigenvalues, and there are corresponding eigenvectors, so \(V/W\) is a direct sum of two 1-dimensional stable subspaces \(T, T'\) such that \(g\cdot t= \omega t\) for all \(t\in T\) and \(g\cdot t' = \omega^2 t'\) for all \(t'\in T'\text{.}\)
We conclude that every real representation of \(C_3\) is isomorphic to a direct sum of copies of the trivial representation and copies of \(V/W\text{.}\) That is, for any such representation \(U\text{,}\) there is a basis of \(U\text{,}\) \(\{e_\alpha \}_{\alpha\in A}\text{,}\) \(\{e'_\beta, e''_\beta\}_{\beta\in B}\text{,}\) such that \(g\cdot e_\alpha = e_\alpha\text{,}\) \(g\cdot e'_\beta = e''_\beta\text{,}\) and \(g\cdot e''_\beta = - e'_\beta - e''_\beta\text{.}\)
We conclude that every complex representation of \(C_3\) is isomorphic to a direct sum of copies of the trivial representation, \(T\text{,}\) and \(T'\text{.}\) That is, for any such representation \(U\text{,}\) there is a basis of \(U\text{,}\) \(\{e_\alpha \}_{\alpha\in A}\text{,}\) \(\{e'_\beta\}_{\beta\in B}\text{,}\) \(\{e''_\gamma\}_{\gamma\in C}\text{,}\) such that \(g\cdot e_\alpha = e_\alpha\text{,}\) \(g\cdot e'_\beta = \omega e'_\beta\text{,}\) and \(g\cdot e''_\gamma = e''_\gamma\text{.}\)
