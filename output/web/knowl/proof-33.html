Proof

We have
\begin{equation*} \mathbb{C}[G] \cong \operatorname{Mat}_{n_1}(\mathbb{C}) \times \cdots \times \operatorname{Mat}_{n_m}(\mathbb{C}) \end{equation*}
and \(m\) is the number of irreducible complex representations up to isomorphism. A key point is that the center of the right side is \(\mathbb{C}I_{n_1} \times \cdots \times \mathbb{C}I_{n_m}\text{,}\) which has dimension \(m\) as a complex vector space. Since this ring isomorphism is \(\mathbb{C}\)-linear, it induces a \(\mathbb{C}\)-linear isomorphism of the centers, and thus we just need to show that \(\dim_\mathbb{C}(Z(\mathbb{C}[G]))\) is equal to the number of conjugacy classes.
Let \(C_1, \dots, C_h\) denote the conjugacy classes of \(G\) (i.e., the orbits for the action of \(G\) on itself by conjugation). For each \(i\) set \(z_i = \sum_{g \in C_i} g \in \mathbb{C}[G]\text{.}\) Then for all \(x \in G\text{,}\) \(x z_i x ^{-1} = \sum_{g \in C_i} x g x^{-1} = z_i\) and it follows that \(z_i \in Z(\mathbb{C}[G])\text{.}\) Since that \(z_i\)â€™s are sums of disjoint subsets of a basis of \(\mathbb{C}[G]\text{,}\) they are linearly independent. Now say \(\sum_g c_g g\) belongs to the center. Then for each \(x \in G\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} \sum_g c_g xgx^{-1} = x(\sum_g c_g g)x^{-1} = \sum_g c_g g \end{equation*}
and it follows that \(c_g = c_h\) whenever \(g,h\) are conjugate. This proves that \(Z(\mathbb{C}[G])\) is spanned by \(z_1, \dots, z_h\text{.}\) We conclude that \(h = \dim_\mathbb{C}(Z(\mathbb{C}[G])) = m\text{.}\)
