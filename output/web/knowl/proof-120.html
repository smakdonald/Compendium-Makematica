Proof

Let \(E\) be the field extension of \(\Q\) obtained by adjoining to \(\Q\) all four complex roots of the polynomial \(x^4 + 5\text{.}\)
Let \(F=\Q(\sqrt[4]{20})\text{.}\) Note that by Eisenstein's Criterion using \(p=5\) we see that \(\sqrt[4]{20}\) is the root of an irreducible polynomial \(x^4-20\) in \(\Q\text{.}\) Thus by Theorem 2.31 we see that \([F:\Q]\) is the degree of the minimum polynomial of this extension. However, as \(x^4-20\) is monic and of degree 4, we see that it is the minimal polynomial and thus that \([F:\Q]=4\text{.}\) As \(\sqrt[4]{20}\in\R-\Q\text{,}\) this extension will be contained in the real numbers, and thus that \(F\subseteq\R\text{.}\)
Next, observe
\begin{equation*} \begin{align} \alpha_1+\alpha_4&=e^{\pi i/4}\sqrt[4]{5}+e^{7\pi i/4}\sqrt[4]{5}\\ &=(\cos(\frac{\pi}{4})+i\sin(\frac{\pi}{4}))\sqrt[4]{5}+(\cos(\frac{7\pi}{4})+i\sin(\frac{7\pi}{4}))\sqrt[4]{5}\\ &=(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}+i\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2})\sqrt[4]{5}+(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}-i\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2})\sqrt[4]{5}\\ &=\sqrt{2}\sqrt[4]{5}\\ &=\sqrt[4]{20}, \end{align} \end{equation*}
and thus that \(F\subseteq E\text{.}\)
Note that \(i\) is the root of \(x^2+1\text{,}\) and since \(F\subseteq\R\) and \(i\not\in\R\text{,}\) we see that \(x^2+1\) is irreducible in \(F\) due to it having degree 2. As \(x^2+1\) is monic and degree 2 we see its the minimal polynomial of \([F(i):F]=2\text{.}\) By the Degree Formula, we see that \([F(i):\Q]=[F(i):F][F:\Q]=(2)(4)=8\text{.}\)
Note that
\(\a_1 = e^{\pi i/4}\sqrt[4]{5} = \cos(\frac{\pi}{4})+i\sin(\frac{\pi}{4})\sqrt[4]{5} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\sqrt[4]{5}+i\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\sqrt[4]{5}=\sqrt[4]{20}(1+i)\text{,}\)
\(\a_2 = e^{3\pi i/4}\sqrt[4]{5} = \cos(\frac{3\pi}{4})+i\sin(\frac{3\pi}{4})\sqrt[4]{5} = \frac{-\sqrt{2}}{2}\sqrt[4]{5}-i\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\sqrt[4]{5}=\sqrt[4]{20}(-1+i)\text{,}\)
\(\a_3 = e^{5\pi i/4}\sqrt[4]{5} = \cos(\frac{5\pi}{4})+i\sin(\frac{5\pi}{4})\sqrt[4]{5} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\sqrt[4]{5}+i\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\sqrt[4]{5}=\sqrt[4]{20}(-1-i)\text{,}\) and
\(\a_4 = e^{7\pi i/4}\sqrt[4]{5} = \cos(\frac{7\pi}{4})+i\sin(\frac{7\pi}{4})\sqrt[4]{5} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\sqrt[4]{5}-i\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\sqrt[4]{5}=\sqrt[4]{20}(1-i)\text{,}\) all of which can be written with \(i\) and \(\sqrt[4]{20}\text{.}\) Thus \(E\subseteq F(i)\text{.}\)
We already know that \(\alpha_1+\alpha_4=\sqrt[4]{20}\text{,}\) and thus all that remains is to show that \(i\) can be expressed in terms of the \(\alpha\text{.}\) Observe
\begin{equation*} \begin{align} \alpha_1+\alpha_2&=e^{\pi i/4}\sqrt[4]{5}+e^{3\pi i/4}\sqrt[4]{5}\\ &=(\cos(\frac{\pi}{4})+i\sin(\frac{\pi}{4}))\sqrt[4]{5}+(\cos(\frac{3\pi}{4})+i\sin(\frac{3\pi}{4}))\sqrt[4]{5}\\ &=(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}+i\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2})\sqrt[4]{5}+(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}+i\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2})\sqrt[4]{5}\\ &=\sqrt{2}\sqrt[4]{5}\\ &=\sqrt[4]{20}i, \end{align} \end{equation*}
and thus that \((\alpha_1+\alpha_2)(\alpha_1+\alpha_4)\inv=i\text{.}\) Thus \(F(i)\subseteq E\text{,}\) and \(F(i)=E\text{.}\) Hence \([E:\Q]=8\text{.}\)
