  1. Let \(G\) be a simple group of order \(60=2^2\cdot3\cdot 5.\) By Sylow's Theorem we know that \(n_5|12\) and that \(n_5\equiv 1\mod{5}\text{.}\) Thus the options for \(n_5\) are \(1\) and \(6\text{.}\) Since \(G\) is simple we see that \(n_5=6\text{.}\) As each Sylow \(5\)-subgroup of \(G\) has \(4\) unique elements of order \(5\) and the identity we see that the number of elements or order \(5\) in \(G\) is \((5-1)\cdot 6=24\text{.}\)
  2. Suppose by way of contradiction that \(G\) is a simple group of order \(30\text{.}\) Similarly to above, \(n_5=6\text{,}\) yielding \(24\) elements of order \(5\text{.}\) Now, \(n_3|10\) and \(n_3\equiv 1\mod{3}\text{,}\) so \(n_3=10\text{,}\) yielding far too many elements to fit in \(G\text{.}\)
