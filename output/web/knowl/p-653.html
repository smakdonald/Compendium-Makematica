We have \(\operatorname{End}_{{\mathbb{R}}[C_3]}(W) \cong {\mathbb{R}}\text{.}\) To compute the endomorphism ring of \(V/W\text{,}\) observe that an \({\mathbb{R}}\)-linear endomorphism of \(V/W\) is \({\mathbb{R}}[C_3]\)-linear if and only if it commutes with the action of \(g\text{.}\) We can write any \({\mathbb{R}}\)-linear endomorphism of \(V/W\) as a \(2\times 2\) matrix; for it to commute with \(g\) means it commutes with \(\begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & -1\end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) We have
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c& d \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & -1\end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & -1\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c& d \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} b + c & -a-b+d \\ d -a + c & -b-c \end{bmatrix}, \end{equation*}
so the matrices we seek are of the form
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} a & -c \\ c & a-c \end{bmatrix}= a \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1\end{bmatrix} + c \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & -1\end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
Any pair of matrices in this set commutes (since the two vectorspace generators do) so they form a commutative ring and hence a field by Schurâ€™s Lemma; any matrix in this collection is algebraic over the subring of scalar matrices (since both generators are). It follows that this collection of matrices is isomorphic as a ring to \(\mathbb{C}\text{.}\)
