Case (4) is more complicated. We have \(\operatorname{Aut}(C_3)\) is itself cyclic of order \(2\text{.}\) Switching to additive notation, the map
\begin{equation*} \rho: C_2 \times C_2 \to \operatorname{Aut}(C_3) \end{equation*}
is given as a map
\begin{equation*} \rho: \mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2 \to \mathbb{Z}/2, \end{equation*}
of elementary abelian \(2\)-groups. Thus, \(\rho\) may be represented by a \(1 \times 2\) matrix with entries in \(\mathbb{F}_2\text{;}\) there are \(4\) such maps. But, using linear algebra, if \(\rho_1, \rho_2\) are any two non-trivial maps of this form, then there is an automorhism \(\beta\) of \(\mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2\) such that \(\rho_2 = \rho_1 \circ \beta\text{.}\) Using the Lemma below, this proves that
\begin{equation*} (\mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2) \rtimes_{\rho_1} \mathbb{Z}/2 \cong (\mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2) \rtimes_{\rho_2} \mathbb{Z}/2 \end{equation*}
for any two non-trivial map. Thus, in case (4) we again get just two groups up to isomorphism: the abelian group \(C_3 \times C_2 \times C_2\) and a non-abelian one.
