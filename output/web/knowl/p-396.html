Consider the group \(\operatorname{GL}_2({\mathbb{R}})\) and elements
\begin{equation*} x = \begin{bmatrix} \cos(2 \pi/n) & - \sin(2 \pi/n) \\ \sin(2 \pi/n) & \cos(2 \pi/n) \\ \end{bmatrix} { \text{ and } } y = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
Then one may check that \(x^n = e\text{,}\) \(y^2 = e\) and \(yxyx = e\text{.}\) PropositionÂ 3.33 gives that there is a group homomorphism \(f: D_{2n} \to \operatorname{GL}_2({\mathbb{R}})\) such that \(f(r) = x\) and \(f(s) = y\text{.}\) This is not at all surprising, since the matrix \(x\) represents rotation by \(2 \pi/n\) radians counter-clockwise and \(y\) represents reflection about the \(y\)-axis. Indeed, one could define \(D_{2n}\) as the subgroup of \(\operatorname{GL}_2({\mathbb{R}})\) consisting of elements \(1, x, \dots, x^{n-1}, y, xy \cdots, x^{n-1}y\text{.}\)
