\(\defn\) - Linear Operator.

By a \(\defnn{linear operator}\) (or \(R\)-linear operator) on \(V\) we mean an \(R\)-module endomorphism \(g: V \to V\) of \(V\text{.}\)
\(\rem\).
Let \(R\) be a commutative ring (with \(1 \ne 0\)) and assume \(V\) is a free \(R\)-module of finite rank \(n\text{.}\) Upon choosing an ordered basis \(B = \{b_1, \dots, b_n\}\) of \(V\text{,}\) we may represent \(g\) as a \(n \times n\) matrix \(X = [g]_B^B\) — a key point here is that for operators we usually use the same basis for both the source and target of the map.
\(\exe\) - Representing Bases.
Here is a concrete example. Let \(R = \R\text{,}\) \(V\) the collection of polynomials in \(x\) with coefficients in \(\R\) of degree at most \(m\text{.}\) Let \(D: V \to V\) be the map sending a polynomial to its derivative. The most obvious choice for a bases of \(V\) is \(1, x, \dots, x^m\text{.}\) With respect to this basis, the map \(D\) is represented by the \(n \times n\) matrix
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 3 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \ddots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & m \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
If, when \(m = 2\text{,}\) we instead used \(1, x^2, 3x-7\) as a basis, then the matrix for \(D\) would be
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & \frac{14}{3} & 3 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac23 & 0\\ \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
\(\rem\).
Say \(X = [g]_B^B\) is the square matrix representing \(g\) with respect to \(B\text{.}\) If \(B'\) is another basis of \(V\) and \(Y = [g]_{B'}^{B'}\) is the matrix representing \(g\) with respect to \(B'\) (used for both the source and target of the map), then we have
\begin{equation*} Y = P X P^{-1} \end{equation*}
where \(P = [\id_V]_{B'}^{B}\) is the change of basis matrix. This holds since
\begin{equation*} PXP^{-1} = [\id_V]_{B'}^{B} [g]_B^B [\id_V]_{B}^{B'} = [g]_{B'}^{B'} = Y. \end{equation*}
\(\defn\) - Similar Matrices.
Two \(n \times n\) matrices \(X\) and \(Y\) with entries in a commutative ring \(R\) are called \(\defnn{similar}\) if \(Y = PXP^{-1}\) for some invertible \(n \times n\) matrix \(P\text{.}\)
\(\rem\).
It is easy to decide if two matrices with entries in a field are equivalent — just row reduce them to see if they have the same rank.
But it is in general quite difficult to decide if two square matrices with entries in a field are similar. The operation \(X \mapsto PXP^{-1}\) can in principle be broken down into a sequence of steps by factoring \(P\) into elementary matrices. That is, two square matrices \(X\) and \(Y\) of the same size are similar if and only if one can be obtained from the other by a sequence of operations of the form \(Z \mapsto EZE^{-1}\) where \(E\) is an elementary matrix.
But such a stap amounts to doing a row operation and simultaneously doing the inverse column operation. There is no simple algorithm, such as Gaussian reduction, to determine whether matrices are similar.
\(\prop\) - Linear Operators and Bases.
Let \(R\) be a non-zero commutative ring. Given a linear operator \(g: V \to V\) on a free \(R\)-module \(V\) of finite rank \(n\text{,}\) we have: 1. The matrices representing \(g\) with respect to any two choices of bases of \(V\) are similar 2. If \(X\) represents \(g\) with respect to a basis \(B\) of \(V\) and if \(Y\) is similar to \(X\text{,}\) then there is a basis \(C\) of \(V\) such that the matrix representing \(g\) with respect to \(C\) is \(Y\text{.}\)
Proof..
We proved the first assertion above.
Say \(X = [g]_B^B\) and \(Y = PXP^{-1}\) for some invertible matrix \(P\text{.}\) By Proposition above, \(P = [\id_V]_B^C\) for a (unique) new basis \(C\text{.}\) So \(Y = [\id]^C_{B} [g]_B^B [\id_V]_C^{B} = [g]_{C}^C\text{.}\)
Problem 5 - Eigenvalues and Linear Maps.
Suppose that \(T : V\to V\) is a linear map, where \(V\) is a finite dimensional \(\C\)-vector space. Fix a polynomial \(p\in \C[x]\text{.}\)
  1. Prove that if \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(T\) then \(p(\lambda)\) is an eigenvalue of \(p(T)\text{.}\)
  2. Prove conversely that if \(\mu\) is an eigenvalue of \(p(T)\) then there exists an eigenvalue \(\l\) of \(T\) such that \(\mu = p(\l)\text{.}\)
Proof..
Part (a).
Let \(\l\) be an eigenvalue of \(T\text{.}\) Thus there exists some \(v\) such that \(Tv=\l v\text{.}\) Notice that \(p(T)v=a_nT^nv+\dots+a_0Iv=0\) and \(p(\l)=a_n\l^n+\dots+a_0\text{.}\) Then
\begin{equation*} \begin{align}(p(T)-p(\l I))v&=p(T)v-p(\l I)v\\&=(a_nT^nv+\dots+a_0Iv)-(a_n\l^nv+\dots+a_0v)\\&=a_n(T^n-\l^n)v+\dots a_0(I-I)v\\&=0,\end{align} \end{equation*}
making \(p(\l)\) an eigenvalue of \(p(T)\text{.}\)
Part (b).
First note that if \(T\) is a scalar of the identity matrix then its only eigenvalue is \(0\text{.}\) Let \(\mu\) be an eigenvalue of \(p(T)\text{.}\) Thus \((p(T)-\mu I)v=0\) for some \(v\text{.}\) As \(\C\) is algebraically closed we can factor the polynomial \(p(x)-\mu\) into linear terms:
\begin{equation*} p(x)-\mu=c(x-r_1)\dots(x-r_n) \end{equation*}
for roots \(r_i\text{.}\) Note that \(p(r_i)-\mu=0\) for all \(r_i\text{.}\) Observe
\begin{equation*} \begin{align}(p(T)-\mu I)v&=p(T)v-(\mu I)v\\&=c(T-r_nI)\dots(T-r_1I)v\\&=0.\end{align} \end{equation*}
Thus one of these terms must be sent to zero. Note that if \(T-r_iI=0\) for any \(I\) this would make \(T\) a scaler of the identity matrix. Thus there exists some \(i\) such that \((T-r_iI)v=0\text{,}\) making \(r_i\) an eigenvalue of \(T\text{.}\) Notice that as \(p(r_i)-\mu=0\) we have \(p(r_i)=\mu\text{,}\) completing the proof.

Show that the principal ideal \((f(x))\) is a subspace of the \(F\)-vector space \(F[x]\text{.}\) 2. Show that the set \(B=\{\ov{1},\ov{x},\ldots,\ov{x^{n-1}}\}\text{,}\) where \(\ov{x^i}=x^i+(f(x))\text{,}\) is a basis for the quotient \(F\)-vector space \(F[x]/(f(x))\text{.}\) 3. Consider the linear transformation \(\l_x : F [x]/(f (x)) \to F[x]/(f(x))\) defined by \(\l_x(v)=\ov{x}v\) for any \(v\in F [x]/(f (x))\text{.}\) Find the matrix representing \(\l_x\) in the basis \(B\) from part b).
Problem.
Let \(F\) be a field, let \(V\) and \(W\) be vector spaces over \(F\text{,}\) let \(a\!: V \to V\) and \(b\!:W \to W\) be linear transformations and let \(V_a\) and \(W_b\) be the \(F[x]\)-modules they determine. 1. Show that a function \(g: V_a \to W_b\) is an \(F[x]\)-module homomorphism if and only if 1. \(g: V \to W\) is a linear transformation and 2. \(g \circ a = b \circ g\text{.}\) 2. Suppose that \(V = F^m = W\text{,}\) and let \(A, B \in \M_m(F)\) be the matrices representing the linear transformations \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) respectively, in the standard basis of \(F^m\text{.}\) Show that there is an \(F[x]\)-module isomorphism \(V_a \cong W_b\) if and only if the matrices \(A\) and \(B\) are similar.
