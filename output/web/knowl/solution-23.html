Solution 3.4.5.1.
Let \(x\in N\) and let \(g\in G\text{.}\) As \(N\) is normal, we see \(gxg\inv\in N\text{,}\) and thus one of the following must be true: - \(gxg\inv=e\text{,}\) where \(e\) is the identity element of \(G\text{,}\) or - \(gxg\inv=x\text{,}\) the only other element of \(N\text{.}\) However, in the first case we would have \(x=e\) by multiplying \(g\) and \(g\inv\) over, and thus it must be the case that \(gxg\inv=x\text{,}\) or \(gx=xg\text{.}\) Thus \(x\in Z(G)\text{.}\)
From Lagrange's Theorem we know that \(|G|/|H|=2\text{,}\) meaning that \(G\) has an even number of elements. Thus \(2\) is the smallest prime dividing the order of \(G\text{,}\) making \(H\) normal in \(G\) (See: ).
Suppose by way of contradiction there exists some non-trivial proper normal subgroup \(N\) of \(G\) that is not \(H\text{.}\) As \(H\) is normal, by the Second Isomorphism Theorem we have \(H\cap N\nsg H\text{.}\) However, as \(H\) is simple, this means that \(H\cap N=\{e\}\text{.}\) SIT also tells us that \(HN\leq G\text{.}\) As \(|H|=|G|/2\text{,}\) this makes \(|N|=2\) and \(G=HN\text{.}\) From part (a), \(N\leq Z(G)\text{.}\) However, this contradicts Lagrange's Theorem, as \(2\) does not divide any odd numbers.
