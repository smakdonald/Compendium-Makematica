Example 6.7.

Let \(k = \mathbb{C}\) and consider the alternating group \(G = A_4\) of order 12. We find all the simple modules over the ring \(\mathbb{C}[A_4]\) or, equivalently, all irreducible \(\mathbb{C}\)-linear representations of \(A_4\text{.}\) We also find the Artin-Wedderburn decomposition of \(\mathbb{C}[A_4]\text{.}\)
As before we start by finding 1-dimensional representations given by group homomorphisms of the form \(A_4 \to \mathbb{C}^\times\text{.}\) Any such map factors as
\begin{equation*} A_4 \twoheadrightarrow A_4^{\mathrm{ab}}\cong C_3 \to \mathbb{C}^\times \end{equation*}
and thus there are three nonisomorphic 1-dimensional representations given by \(\rho_i:C_3=\langle g\rangle \to \mathbb{C}^\times\text{,}\) \(\rho_i(g)=e^\frac{2\pi i}{3}\text{,}\) with \(i=0,1,2\text{.}\) Note that \(\rho_0\) corresponds to the trivial representation. Also \(\rho_1\) and \(\rho_2\) make essential use of the fact that we are working over \(\mathbb{C}\) as opposed to, say, \({\mathbb{R}}\) where there are no primitive cubic roots ofÂ 1.
With respect to the Artin-Wedderburn decomposition we have so far
\begin{equation*} \mathbb{C}[A_4] \cong \mathbb{C}\times \mathbb{C}\times \mathbb{C}\times \operatorname{Mat}_{n_4}(\mathbb{C})\times \cdots \times \operatorname{Mat}_{n_m}(\mathbb{C}). \end{equation*}
where \(n_3, \ldots, n_m\geq 2\) because we have already found all the 1-dimensional representations (\(n_i=1\)) above. Counting dimensions we obtain
\begin{equation*} 12= 1+ 1 + 1 +\sum_{i=4}^m {n_i}^2. \end{equation*}
It is easy to see there is only one solution: \(m=4\) and \(n_4=3\text{.}\) Hence there is a unique up to isomorphism \(\mathbb{C}\)-linear irreducible representation of \(A_4\) which is a 3 dimensional \(\mathbb{C}\)-vector space.
To exhibit such a representation, let \(A_4\) act on \(V=\mathbb{C}^4\) by permuting the standard basis elements and thus any vector in \(V\text{.}\) The subspace \(W\subseteq V\) given by
\begin{equation*} W = \{ (a,b,c,d) \ | \ a+b+c+d=0\} \end{equation*}
is an \(A_4\)-stable subspace. This is an irreducible representation: if \(v\in W\smallsetminus 0\text{,}\) after permuting and scaling, we can write \(v=(1,x,y,-1-x-y)\text{.}\) We also have \((1,-1-x-y,x,y)\) and \((1,y,-1-x-y,x)\) in \(\langle v \rangle\text{,}\) so the sum \((3,-1,-1,-1)\in \langle v \rangle\text{.}\) Then \((-1,3,-1,-1)\) and \((-1,-1,3,-1)\) are also in \(\langle v \rangle\text{,}\) and these are three linearly independent vectors, so we must have \(\langle v \rangle=W\text{.}\)
