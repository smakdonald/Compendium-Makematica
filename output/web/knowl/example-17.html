Example 28.9.

The group ring \(R=\mathbb{F}_p[C_p]\) does not satisfy the hypotheses of Maschkeâ€™s theorem, since the order of the group is zero in the field. In fact, \(\mathbb{F}_p[C_p]\) is not semisimple: let \(V=\mathbb{F}_p^2\) be the \({C_p= \langle g\rangle}\) representation \(g^n \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ \lambda n & 1\end{bmatrix}\text{;}\) i.e., as a \(\mathbb{F}_p[C_p]\)-module, we have \(g \cdot \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} a \\ a+b\end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) We claim that \({U=\left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ b \end{bmatrix}\right\}}\) is the unique nonzero proper submodule of \(V\text{.}\) Let \(W\subseteq V\) be a nonzero submodule and suppose that \(U\neq W\text{.}\) Then, there is some element \(v=\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b\end{bmatrix} \in W\) with \(a\neq 0\text{.}\) Then \(v\) and \(gv\) are linearly independent, so we must have \(W=V\text{.}\) It follows that \(V\) is not semisimple: it is not simple since \(0\subsetneqq U \subsetneqq V\text{,}\) but \(V\) is not a direct sum of simple modules.
