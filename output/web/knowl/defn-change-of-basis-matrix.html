\(\defn\) – Change of Basis Matrix.

Let \(V\) be a finite rank free module over a commutative ring \(R\text{,}\) and let \(B\) and \(B'\) be bases of \(V\text{.}\) Let \(\id_V\) be the identity map on \(V\text{.}\) Then \([\id_V]_B^{B'}\) is called the \(\defnn{change of basis matrix}\) from \(B\) to \(B'.\)
\(\rem\).
By Proposition, \([\id_V]_B^{B'}\) is an invertible matrix and its inverse
\begin{equation*} \begin{equation} \left([\id_V]_B^{B'}\right)^{-1}=[\id_V]_{B'}^B. \end{equation} \end{equation*}
It follows from that
\begin{equation*} [\id_V]_B^{B'} \cdot [v]^B = [v]^{B'}; \end{equation*}
that is, multiplication by \([\id_V]_B^{B'}\) really does change the representation of vectors by column vectors from one basis to another.
\(\exe\) – CoB Matrix and Standard Free Module.
Say \(V = R^n\text{,}\) \(B\) is the standard basis (so \(b_i\) is a column vector with a \(1\) in the \(i\)-th position and \(0\)’s elsewhere). Note that \([v]^B = v\) for all \(v\text{.}\)
Let \(B' = \{b'_1, \dots, b'_b\}\) be any other basis of \(V\text{.}\) Then \([b'_i]^B = b_i\) and so the change of basis matrix \([\id_V]_{B'}^{B}\) satisfies, on the one hand,
\begin{equation*} [\id_V]_{B'}^{B} [b'_i]^{B'}=[\id_V]_{B'}^{B} b_i = \text{the $i$-th column of $[\id_V]_{B'}^{B}$} \end{equation*}
and, on the other hand,
\begin{equation*} [\id_V]_{B'}^{B} [b'_i]^{B'}=[b'_i]^B = b'_i. \end{equation*}
That is, \([\id_V]_{B'}^B\) is the matrix whose columns are \(b_1', \dots, b_n'\text{.}\)
\(\exe\) – CoB and \(P_n\).
Consider \(V = P_2\text{,}\) let \(B = \{1, x, x^2\}\) and \(B' = \{1,x-2,(x-2)^2\}\) be bases of \(V\text{.}\) We calculate the change of basis matrix. We have
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}\id_V(1) &=1 ,\\ \id_V(x) &=2\cdot1+1\cdot(x-2), \\ \id_V(x^2) &=4\cdot1 +4\cdot(x-2)+1\cdot(x-2)^2. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
Thus, the change of basis matrix is given by
\begin{equation*} [\id_V]_B^{B'} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 4\\ 0 & 1 & 4\\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
If we wish to represent \(f(x) = 1 + x + x^2\) in the basis \(B'\text{,}\) we start by noting \([f(x)]^B = (1,1,1)^\tau\) (where recall \(\tau\) means to take the transpose) and compute \([\id_V]_B^{B'} \cdot (1,1,1)^\tau\) to get \((7, 5, 1)^\tau\text{.}\) This tells us that
\begin{equation*} 1 + x + x^2 = 7 + 5(x-2) + (x-2)^2. \end{equation*}
\(\prop\) – CoB and Identity.
Let \(V, W\) be finitely generated free modules over a commutative ring \(R\text{,}\) let \(B\) and \(B'\) be bases of \(V\text{,}\) let \(C\) and \(C'\) be bases of \(W\text{,}\) and let \(f: V \to W\) be a homomorphism. Then[^1]
\begin{equation*} \begin{equation}[f]_{B'}^{C'} = [\id_W]_C^{C'} [f]_B^C [\id_V]_{B'}^{B}.\end{equation} \end{equation*}
Proof..
This is seen to hold by applying twice (since \(\id_W \circ f \circ \id_V = f\)).
\(\defn\) – Equivalent Matrices.
Two \(m \times n\) matrices \(X\) and \(Y\) are \(\defnn{equivalent}\) if there exists invertible matrices \(P\) and \(Q\) (of the appropriate sizes) so that \(Y = PXQ\text{.}\)
\(\rem\).
With this bit of terminology, we can say that two matrices representing the same linear transformation with respect to a different pair of bases are equivalent. The converse is also true: If two matrices are equivalent, then they represent the same linear transformation with respect to different bases.
\(\thm\) – Finding Equivalent Matrices.
Let \(F\) be a field. Given an \(F\)-linear transformation \(g: V \to U\) between finite dimensional \(F\)-vector spaces \(V\) and \(U\text{,}\) there are bases \(B\) and \(C\) of \(V\) and \(U\text{,}\) respectively, such that[^1]
\begin{equation*} [g]_B^C = \begin{bmatrix} I_r & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \\ \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
where \(r = \rank(g)\)[^2] and the \(0\)’s denote appropriately sized matrices with all \(0\) entries.
Proof..
We form \(B\) and \(C\) in steps.
Start by picking an ordered basis \(C' = \{w_1, \dots, w_r\}\) of the image of \(g\text{.}\) For each \(i\) pick \(v_i \in V\) such that \(g(v_i) = w_i\) and set \(B' = \{v_1, \dots, v_r\}\text{.}\) Then pick a basis \(B''\) of the kernel of \(g\text{.}\) Let us list the elements of \(B''\) as \(\{v_{r+1}, \dots, v_n\}\text{.}\)
I claim that \(B := B' \cup B'' = \{v_1, \dots, v_n\}\) is a basis of \(V\text{.}\) (Note that \(B' \cap B'' = \emptyset\) since \(g(v_i) = w_i \ne 0\) for all \(i\) with \(1 \leq i \leq r\) and \(g(v_i) = 0\) for all \(i > r\text{.}\)) Pick \(v \in V\text{.}\) Then, since \(C'\) spans the image of \(g\text{,}\) we have \(g(v) = \sum_{i=1}^r a_i w_i\) for some scalars \(a_i \in F\text{.}\) It follows that \(v - \sum_{i=1}^r a_i v_i \in \ker(g)\) and hence, since the kernel is spanned by \(B''\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation*} v - \sum_{i=1}^r a_i v_i = \sum_{i=r+1}^n a_i v_i, \end{equation*}
for some \(a_{r+1}, \dots, a_n \in F\text{.}\) We rearranging this equation, we conclude \(v \in \Span(B)\) and thus \(B\) spans \(V\text{.}\)
Now say \(\sum_{i=1}^n c_i v_i = 0\) for some \(c_i\)’s in \(F\text{.}\) Since \(g(v_i) = 0\) for all \(i > r\) and \(g(v_i) = w_i\) for all \(i \leq r\text{,}\) this gives that \(0 = g(0) = g(\sum_{i=1}^n c_i v_i) = \sum_{i=1}^n c_i g(v_i) =\sum_{i=1}^r c_i w_i = 0\text{.}\) Since \(C'\) is linearly independent, we have \(c_i = 0\) for \(1 \leq i \leq r\text{.}\) Going back to the original equation
\(\sum_{i=1}^n c_i v_i = 0\text{,}\) we see that \(\sum_{i=1}^r c_i v_i = 0\) and hence \(c_i=0\) for all \(i\text{,}\) since \(B''\) is linearly independent.
Finally we extend \(C'\) to an ordered basis \(C = \{w_1, \dots, w_r, w_{r+1}, \dots, w_m\}\) of \(W\) arbitrarily.
By our construction, for any \(v_i \in B\) we have
\begin{equation*} g(v_i) = \begin{cases} w_i & \text{for $1 \leq i \leq r$ and} \\ 0 & \text{for $r+1 \leq i \leq n$} \\ \end{cases} \end{equation*}
and hence
\begin{equation*} [g]_B^C = \begin{bmatrix} I_r & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \\ \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
Note that, in our construction, \(r = \dim_F(\im(g)) = \rank(g)\text{.}\)[^3]
\(\cor\) – Every \(m\times n\) Matrix Equivalent to Unique Matrix.
Every \(m \times n\) matrix \(A\) with entries in a field is equivalent to a unique matrix of the form
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} I_r & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \\ \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
where \(r\) is the rank of \(A\text{.}\)
Proof..
Let \(V = F^n\) and \(W = F^m\) and \(g: V \to W\) be the linear transformation given by \(g(v) = A \cdot v\text{,}\) matrix multiplication. (I have called this map \(T_A\) in the past.) If \(B'\) and \(C'\) are the standard basis of \(V\) and \(W\) then \([g]_{B'}^{C'} = A\text{.}\)
The Theorem gives that there are bases \(B\) and \(C\) of \(V\) and \(W\) such that
\begin{equation*} [g]_B^C = \begin{bmatrix} I_r & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \\ \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
So, by Proposition , we have
\begin{equation*} A = [g]_{B'}^{C'} = [\id_W]_C^{C'}[g]_B^C [\id_V]_{B'}^{B}=P \begin{bmatrix}I_r & 0 \\0 & 0 \\\end{bmatrix}Q \end{equation*}
with \(P := [\id_W]_C^{C'}\) and \(Q := [\id_V]_{B'}^{B}\text{.}\) Note that \(P, Q\) are invertible since they are change of basis matrices (see ).
The uniqueness of \(r\) follows from the fact that \(r = \rank_F(g)\text{,}\) which does not depend on a choice of bases.
\(\prop\) – Finding Unique Equivalent Matrix.
Let \(R\) be a non-zero commutative ring, let \(V\) be a free \(R\)-module of dimension \(n\text{,}\) and let \(B = \{b_1, \dots, b_n\}\) be any ordered basis of \(V\text{.}\) If \(P\) is any \(n \times n\) matrix with entries in \(R\) that is invertible (i.e., there is another \(n \times n\) matrix \(P^{-1}\) such that \(PP^{-1} = I_n = P^{-1}P\)), then \(P = [\id_V]_B^C\)[^1] for a unique basis \(C\) of \(V\text{.}\)
Proof..
Each of the maps
\begin{equation*} V \xra{v \mapsto [v]^B} R^n \xra{w \mapsto P^{-1} \cdot w} R^n \xra{(r_1, \dots, r_n)^\tau \mapsto \sum_i r_i b_i} V \end{equation*}
is an isomorphism. (The one on the far right is the inverse of the one on the far left. The middle one is since \(u \mapsto P \cdot u\) is a two-sided inverse.) So the composition of all three of these maps, let us call it \(g\text{,}\) is also an isomorphism. It follows that \(C = \{g(b_1), \dots, g(b_n)\}\) is a basis of \(V\text{.}\) I leave it as an exercise for you to check that \(P = [\id_V]_B^C\text{.}\)
To show the uniqueness, say \(C = \{c_1, \dots, c_n\}\text{,}\) say \(C' = \{c'_1, \dots, c'_n\}\) are ordered bases such that \([\id_V]_B^{C'} = P = [\id_V]_B^C\text{.}\) Then
\begin{equation*} [\id_V]_C^{C'} = [\id_V]_B^{C'} [\id_V]_C^B = [\id_V]_B^{C'} \left( [\id_V]_B^{C} \right)^{-1} = PP^{-1} = I_n. \end{equation*}
For each \(i\) we have
\begin{equation*} [c_i]^C = (0, \dots, 0, 1, 0, \dots, 0)^\tau \end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*} [c_i]^C = I_n [c_i]^C = [\id_V]_C^{C'} [c_i]^C = [c_i]^{C'} \end{equation*}
so that
\begin{equation*} [c_i]^{C'} = (0, \dots, 0, 1, 0, \dots, 0)^\tau \end{equation*}
which gives \(c_i = c_i'\text{.}\)
Problem 5 #unfinished.
Let F be a field, \(V\) and \(W\) finite dimensional \(F\)-vector spaces, and \(g : V\to W\) an\(F\)-linear transformation.
  1. Prove that there exists bases of \(V\) and \(W\) such that the matrix representing \(g\) with respect to these bases has the form
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} I_{r\times r} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}, \end{equation*}
    where \(I_{r\times r}\) is the \(r\times r\) identity matrix and all the \(0\)’s denote zero matrices of the appropriate size.
  2. Prove that the number \(r\) appearing in part (a) is independent of choice of bases; that is, if, for another pair of bases of \(V\) and \(W\text{,}\) the matrix representing \(g\) has the form
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} I_{r'\times r'} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    then \(r'=r\text{.}\)
Proof..
Let F be a field, \(V\) and \(W\) finite dimensional \(F\)-vector spaces, and \(g : V\to W\) an\(F\)-linear transformation.
Part (a).
We form \(B\) and \(C\) in steps.
Start by picking an ordered basis \(C' = \{w_1, \dots, w_r\}\) of the image of \(g\text{.}\) For each \(i\) pick \(v_i \in V\) such that \(g(v_i) = w_i\) and set \(B' = \{v_1, \dots, v_r\}\text{.}\) Then pick a basis \(B''\) of the kernel of \(g\text{.}\) Let us list the elements of \(B''\) as \(\{v_{r+1}, \dots, v_n\}\text{.}\)
I claim that \(B := B' \cup B'' = \{v_1, \dots, v_n\}\) is a basis of \(V\text{.}\) (Note that \(B' \cap B'' = \emptyset\) since \(g(v_i) = w_i \ne 0\) for all \(i\) with \(1 \leq i \leq r\) and \(g(v_i) = 0\) for all \(i > r\text{.}\)) Pick \(v \in V\text{.}\) Then, since \(C'\) spans the image of \(g\text{,}\) we have \(g(v) = \sum_{i=1}^r a_i w_i\) for some scalars \(a_i \in F\text{.}\) It follows that \(v - \sum_{i=1}^r a_i v_i \in \ker(g)\) and hence, since the kernel is spanned by \(B''\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation*} v - \sum_{i=1}^r a_i v_i = \sum_{i=r+1}^n a_i v_i, \end{equation*}
for some \(a_{r+1}, \dots, a_n \in F\text{.}\) We rearranging this equation, we conclude \(v \in \Span(B)\) and thus \(B\) spans \(V\text{.}\)
Now say \(\sum_{i=1}^n c_i v_i = 0\) for some \(c_i\)’s in \(F\text{.}\) Since \(g(v_i) = 0\) for all \(i > r\) and \(g(v_i) = w_i\) for all \(i \leq r\text{,}\) this gives that \(0 = g(0) = g(\sum_{i=1}^n c_i v_i) = \sum_{i=1}^n c_i g(v_i) =\sum_{i=1}^r c_i w_i = 0\text{.}\) Since \(C'\) is linearly independent, we have \(c_i = 0\) for \(1 \leq i \leq r\text{.}\) Going back to the original equation
\(\sum_{i=1}^n c_i v_i = 0\text{,}\) we see that \(\sum_{i=1}^r c_i v_i = 0\) and hence \(c_i=0\) for all \(i\text{,}\) since \(B''\) is linearly independent.
Finally we extend \(C'\) to an ordered basis \(C = \{w_1, \dots, w_r, w_{r+1}, \dots, w_m\}\) of \(W\) arbitrarily.
By our construction, for any \(v_i \in B\) we have
\begin{equation*} g(v_i) = \begin{cases} w_i & \text{for $1 \leq i \leq r$ and} \\ 0 & \text{for $r+1 \leq i \leq n$} \\ \end{cases} \end{equation*}
and hence
\begin{equation*} [g]_B^C = \begin{bmatrix} I_r & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \\ \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
