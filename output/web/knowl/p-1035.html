Define a funtion
\begin{equation*} i: H \to H \rtimes_\rho K \end{equation*}
as \(i(y) = (y, e)\text{.}\) Then \(i\) is a homomorphism, since
\begin{equation*} i(y_1) i(y_2) = (y_1,e)(y_2,e) = (y_1\rho(e)(y_2) , ee) = (y_1 y_2, e) = i(y_1y_2). \end{equation*}
The map is clearly injective and hence its image is isomorphic to \(H\text{.}\) In fact, the image is normal since the second component of
\begin{equation*} (y,x) (y_2, e) (\rho(x^{-1})(y^{-1}), x^{-1}) \end{equation*}
is clearly \(e\text{.}\) Let us write this image as
\begin{equation*} H' := \{(y,e) \mid y \in H\} \unlhd H \rtimes_\rho K. \end{equation*}
