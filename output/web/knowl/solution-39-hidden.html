Let \(G\) be a group of order \(385 = 5 · 7 · 11\text{.}\) By Sylow's Theorem we know the \(n_{11}|35\) and \(n_11\equiv 1\mod{11}\text{,}\) and so \(n_{11}=1\text{,}\) making \(P\text{,}\) the unique Sylow \(11\)-subgroup of \(G\text{,}\) normal in \(G\text{.}\) Things are looking good thus far. We also know the following: - \(n_7|55\) and \(n_7\equiv 1\mod{7}\text{,}\) so \(n_7=1\) as well. Yeehaw.
Let \(P\) denote the unique Sylow \(7\)-subgroup, and let \(G\) act on \(P\) by conjugation. Thus \(\rho:G\to S_7\text{.}\) The First Isomorphism Theorem tells us that \(G/K\cong\im(\rho)\leq\Aut(P)\text{,}\) where \(K\) is the kernel of \(\rho\text{.}\) However, \(\Aut(P)=\Z_{7}^\times=\Z_6\text{,}\) meaning that the order of \(K\) must divide both \(385\) and \(6\text{,}\) which cannot happen. Thus \(K\) must be trivial, meaning that \(gxg\inv=x\) for every \(g\in G\) and \(x\in P\text{,}\) making \(P\) a subgroup of \(Z(G)\) of order \(7\text{.}\)
