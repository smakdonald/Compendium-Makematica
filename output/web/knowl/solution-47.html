Solution 5.3.12.1.
By Sylow's Theorem we know that \(\Syl_7(G)\equiv 1\mod{7}\) and \(\Syl_7(G)|2^5=32\text{.}\) Thus our options are \(1\) and \(8\text{.}\) Suppose that \(\Syl_7(G)=8\text{.}\)
Let \(G\) act on \(\Syl_7(G)\) by Conjugation, yielding the homomorphism
\begin{equation*} \rho: G \to S_8 \end{equation*}
via the permutation representation. This map is non-trivial from part (2) of Sylow's Theorem, but \(2^5\cdot 7^3\) does not divide \(|S_8|=8!\text{,}\) and thus \(\rho\) cannot be injective. Then the kernel of this homomorphism is non-trivial, normal subgroup of \(G\text{.}\) Thus \(G\) is not simple.
