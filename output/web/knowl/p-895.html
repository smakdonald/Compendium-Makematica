Suppose by way of contradiction that \(G\) is simple. By Sylow's Theorem we know the following: - \(n_3|10\) and is congruent to \(1\mod{3}\text{.}\) As \(G\) is simple there must be ten of the fuckers. - \(n_5|18\) and is congruent to \(1\mod 5\text{.}\) As \(G\) is simple there must be six of them. Since each Sylow \(3\)-subgroup will have \(3^2-1=8\) non-identity elements and they are all distinct that accounts for \(80\) elements of order \(3\text{.}\) However, there are also \(24\) elements or order \(5\) to account for, which is a problem. Thus \(G\) cannot be simple.
