\(\defn\) – Elementary Basis Change Operations.

Let \(R\) be a commutative ring with \(1 \neq 0\text{,}\) let \(M\) be a free \(R\)-module of finite rank \(n\text{,}\) and let \(B = \{b_1,\dots ,b_n\}\) be an ordered basis for \(M\text{.}\) An \(\defnn{elementary basis change operation}\) on the basis \(B\) is one of the following three types of operations: 1. (Type I) Replacing \(b_i\) by \(b_i + rb_j\) for some \(i \neq j\) and some \(r\in R\text{.}\) 2. (Type II) Replacing \(b_i\) by \(ub_i\) for some \(i\) and some unit \(u\) of \(R\text{,}\) 3. (Type III) Swapping the positions of \(b_i\) and \(b_j\) for some \(i \neq j\text{.}\)
\(\defn\) – Elementary Row Operations.
Let \(R\) be a commutative ring with \(1 \neq 0\text{.}\) An \(\defnn{elementary row (column) operation}\) on a matrix \(A \in \Mat_{m,n}(R)\) is one of the following three types of operations: 1. (Type I) Adding an element of \(R\) times a row (column) of \(A\) to a different row column of \(A\text{.}\) 2. (Type II) Multiplying a row (column) of \(A\) by a unit of \(R\text{.}\) 3. (Type III) Interchanging two rows (columns) of \(A\text{.}\)
\(\defn\) – Elementary Matrix.
Let \(R\) be a commutative ring with \(1 \neq 0\text{.}\) An \(\defnn{elementary matrix}\) over \(R\) is an \(n \times n\) matrix obtained from \(I_n\) by applying a single elementary column operation (or, equivalently, a single elementary column operation). In more detail: 1. (Type I) For \(r \in R\) and \(1 \leq i,j \leq n\) with \(i \neq j\text{,}\) let \(E_{i,j}(r)\) be the type I elementary matrix with \(1\)’s on the diagonal, \(r\) in the \((i,j)\) position, and \(0\) everywhere else. 2. (Type II) For \(u \in R^\times\) and \(1\leq i \leq n\) let \(E_i(u)\) be the type II elementary matrix with \((i,i)\) entry \(u\text{,}\) \((j,j)\) entry \(1\) for all \(j \neq i\text{,}\) and \(0\) everywhere else. 3. Type III) For \(1 \leq i,j \leq n\) with \(i \neq j\text{,}\) let \(E_{(i,j)}\) be the type III elementary matrix with \(1\) in the \((i,j)\) and \((j,i)\) positions and in the \((l,l)\) positions for all \(l\not \in \{i,j\}\text{,}\) and 0 in all other entries.
\(\rem\).
In particular, the Lemma and the fact that elementary matrices are invertible shows that if \(A\) is an \(n \times n\) invertible matrix, then the result of performing elementary row or column operations on \(A\) results in another invertible matrix.
\(\lem\) – Properties of Elementary Matrices.
Let \(E\) be an \(n \times n\) elementary matrix. - For a free \(R\)-module \(V\) with basis \(B\) such that \(|B| = n\text{,}\) \(E\) is the change of basis matrix \([\id_V]_{B'}^{B}\text{,}\) where \(B'\) is the basis obtained from \(B\) by the corresponding elementary basis change operation. - If \(A \in \Mat_{n,q}(R)\text{,}\) then the product matrix \(E \cdot A\) is the result of performing the corresponding elementary column operation on \(A\text{.}\) - If \(B \in \Mat_{m,n}(R)\text{,}\) then the product matrix \(B \cdot E\) is the result of performing the corresponding elementary column operation on \(B\text{.}\)
\(\rem\).
This is true for commutative rings in general, but the proof is much harder.
\(\lem\) – Matrix Invertible iff Columns Span \(F^n\).
Let \(F\) be a field and let \(A\) be an \(n \times n\) matrix for \(n \geq 1\text{.}\) \(A\) is invertible if and only if its columns span \(F^n\text{.}\)
Proof..
\(A\) is invertible if and only if the associated linear map \(g: F^n \to F^n\) given by \(g(v) = Av\) is an isomorphism. By the rank-nullity Theorem, \(\rank(g) = n\) if and only if \(\ker(g) = \{0\}\) if and only if \(g\) is an isomorphism. The result follows, since \(\rank(g) = \dim_F \im(g)\) and \(\im(g)\) is the span of the columns of \(A\text{.}\)
\(\rem\).
The previous proof used the following fact implicitly:
If \(W\) is a subspace of a finite dimensional \(F\)-vector space \(V\) (where \(F\) is a field) and \(\dim_F(W) = \dim_F(V)\text{,}\) then \(W = V\text{.}\)
This holds since any basis of \(W\) can be extended to a basis of \(V\) and hence if \(W\) is properly contained in \(V\) we must have \(\dim_F(W) < \dim_F(V)\text{.}\)
This fact fails for commutative rings in general. For instance, let \(V = \Z\) and \(W = \Z \cdot 2\text{.}\) Then \(V\) and \(W\) are free of rank one and \(W\) is a submodule of \(V\text{,}\) but \(W \ne V\text{.}\)
\(\thm\) – Gauss.
Let \(F\) be a field and let \(A\) be an \(n \times n\) invertible matrix for \(n \geq 1\text{.}\) Then \(A\) can be transformed to a matrix of the form
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} u & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \ddots & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 1 \\ \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
for some \(u \ne 0\) via elementary column and column operations of type I.
Proof..
Proceed by induction on \(n\text{.}\) If \(n = 1\text{,}\) there is nothing to prove (since \(A\) cannot be the \(0\) matrix.) Assume \(n > 1\) and that the result holds for \(n-1\) by \(n-1\) invertible matrices.
Let \(A = (a_{i,j})\text{.}\) Since \(A\) is invertible, its column space must have dimension \(n\text{,}\) by the previous Lemma, and in particular none of its columns can be \(0\) (since otherwise the column space would be spanned by \(n-1\) vectors and would thus have dimension at most \(n-1\)). In particular, its second-to-last column has at least one non-zero entry. By either doing nothing or by adding a suitable row to the last row, we can assume \(a_{n,n-1} \ne 0\text{.}\) By adding \((1 -a_{n,n}) a_{n,n-1}^{-1}\) times column \(n-1\) to column \(n\text{,}\) we obtain \(a_{n,n} = 1\text{.}\) Next do suitable column operations of type I to arrive at \(a_{n,j} = 0\) for all \(j < n\text{,}\) and finally do row operations of type I to also get \(a_{i,n} = 0\) for all \(i < n\text{.}\) We have thus transformed \(A\) to a matrix of the form \(\begin{bmatrix} A' & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Note that this matrix remains invertible (see Remark ) and so the columns of this matrix span \(F^n\) by the previous Lemma. Since \(a_{n,j} = 0\) for all \(j < n\text{,}\) the columns of \(A'\) must span \(F^{n-1}\text{.}\) Thus \(A'\) is invertible by the previous Lemma. By induction, we can transform \(A'\) via type I row and column operations to a matrix of the form as in the statement. Notice that since the \(a_{n,j} = 0\) for all \(j < n\) and \(a_{i,n} = 0\) for all \(i < n\text{,}\) such operations will also put \(A\) into this form.
\(\cor\) – Invertible Matrices and Identity Matrix.
If \(A\) is invertible then it can be transformed into \(I_n\) via elementary column and column operations of type I and II.
Proof..
Apply the Theorem and then use a single type II operator; i.e., multiply the first row by \(u^{-1}\text{.}\)
\(\cor\) – Square Matrix Invertible iff \(\Pi\) of Elementary Matrices.
If \(F\) is a field, then a square matrix is invertible if and only if it is a product of elementary matrices.
Proof..
(\(\Leftarrow\)) Every elementary matrix is invertible and a product of invertible matrices is again invertible.
(\(\Rightarrow\)) If \(A\) is invertible, then by the previous Corollary and Lemma there exists elementary matrices \(E_1, \dots, E_s\text{,}\) \(F_1, \dots, F_t\) of type I or II such that
\begin{equation*} E_s \cdots E_1 \cdot A \cdot F_1 \cdots F_t= I_n \end{equation*}
and hence \(A = E_1^{-1} \cdots E_s^{-1}F_t^{-1} \cdots F^{-1}_1\text{.}\) This proves the statement since the inverse of an elementary matrix is an elementary matrix.
\(\cor\) – Matrices Equivalent iff Path with Operations.
Given two \(m \times n\) matrices \(A\) and \(B\) with entries in a field, \(A\) and \(B\) are equivalent if and only if each can be transformed to the other via elementary column and column operations.
\(\rem\).
For a ring \(R\text{,}\) recall that \(\GL_n(R)\) is the group of invertible \(n \times n\) matrices with entries in \(R\text{.}\) Let \(E_n(R)\) be the subset of \(\GL_n(R)\) consisting of all products of elementary matrices of type I. Since the inverse of an elementary matrix of type I is again an elementary matrix of type I, \(E_n(R)\) is a subgroup of \(\GL_n(R)\text{.}\) In fact it is a normal subgroup, at least when \(n \geq 3\text{,}\) and hence the quotient group \(\GL_n(R)/E_n(R)\) is defined in this case.
As a consequence of the Theorem above, if \(R = F\) is a field then there is an isomorphism of groups \(\GL_n(F)/E_n(F) \cong F^\times\) where \(F^\times = (F \setminus \{0\}, \cdot)\) is the group of units in \(F\text{.}\)
For a general ring, the extent to which \(\GL_n(R)/E_n(R)\) is “larger’’ than \(R^\times\) measure the extent to which Theorem fails.
The algebraic \(K\)-group \(K_1(R)\) is defined to be \(\GL_\infty(R)/E_\infty(R)\) (and it is usually isomorphic to \(\GL_n(R)/E_n(R)\) for \(n \gg 0\)). Moreover, \(E_\infty(R)\) is the derived subgroup of \(\GL_\infty(R)\text{,}\) and hence \(K_1(R)\) is the abelianization of \(\GL_\infty(R)\text{.}\)
