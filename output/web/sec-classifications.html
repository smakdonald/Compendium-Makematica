    Subsection Groups of Order \(pq\)

    “I did not know I was in my prime until afterwards.”
    ―Mason Cooley

    Theorem 8.9. Groups of Order \(6\).

    Any group of order \(6\) is isomorphic either to \(C_6\) or to \(D_6\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Let \(G\) be a group of order \(6\text{.}\) Cayley's theorem gives that there exist elements \(x,y\in G\) with \(|x|=2\) and \(|y|=3\text{.}\) Let \(K=\langle x \rangle\) and \(H=\langle y\rangle\text{.}\) Since \([G:H]=2\text{,}\) \(H\) is a normal subgroup of \(G\) and since \(H\cap K\) is a common subgroup of \(H\) and \(K\) Lagrange's Theorem gives that \(|H\cap K|\mid \operatorname{gcd}(|H|,|K|)=1\text{.}\) Thus \(H\cap K=\{e\}\) and since \(|HK|=\frac{|H||K|}{|H\cap K|}=6=|G|\) we deduce that \(HK=G\text{.}\) Proposition [provisional cross-reference: prop:internaldirprod] now gives that \(G\) is the internal semidirect product of \(H\) and \(K\text{.}\) More to the point, \(G\cong H\rtimes_\rho K\text{,}\) where \(\rho:K\to \operatorname{Aut}(H)\) gives the action of \(K\) on \(H\) by Conjugation.
    We now analyze the possibilities for \(\rho\text{.}\) By Lemma Theorem 3.48, \(\operatorname{Aut}(H)\cong\operatorname{Aut}(\mathbb{Z}/3)\cong (\mathbb{Z}/3^\times, \cdot)=(\{\pm 1\},\cdot)\text{.}\) There are two possibilities for the image of \(\rho\text{:}\) either \(\operatorname{Im}(\rho)=\{\text{id}_H\}\) or \(\operatorname{Im}(\rho)=\operatorname{Aut}(H)\text{.}\)
    If \(\operatorname{Im}(\rho)=\{\text{id}_H\}\text{,}\) then \(\rho(x)=c_x=\text{id}_H\) (which implies \(xy=yx\)) and \(H\rtimes_\rho K=H\times K\text{.}\) Therefore, in this case \(G\cong H\times K\cong C_3\times C_2\cong C_6\text{,}\) where the last isomorphism uses the Chinese Remainder Theorem [provisional cross-reference: thm-sunzi-remainder-thm].
    If \(\operatorname{Im}(\rho)=\operatorname{Aut}(H)\text{,}\) then \(\rho(x)\) is the map \(y^i\mapsto y^{-1}\) and by an earlier example for this \(\rho\) we have \(H\rtimes_\rho K\cong D_6\text{,}\) so \(G\cong D_6\text{.}\)
    Finally, \(C_6\not \cong D_6\) because the former is abelian and the latter is not.
    Let's repeat the previous example for classifying groups of order \(p\cdot q\) with \(p, q\) distinct primes into isomorphism classes.

    Theorem 8.10. Classification for Groups of Order \(pq\).

    Let \(p<q\) be primes.
    1. If \(p\nmid q-1\) there is a unique group of order \(pq\) up to isomorphism, namely \(C_{pq}\text{.}\)
    2. If \(p\mid q-1\) there are exactly two groups of order \(pq\) up to isomorphism, namely \(C_{pq}\) and a non-abelian group.

    Proof.

    Let \(G\) be a group of order \(pq\) and let \(H, K\) be Sylow subgroups of order \(q\) and \(p\) respectively. We see that \(H\) is a normal subgroup using a HW problem, since \([G:H]=p\) is the smallest prime that divides \(|G|\text{.}\)
    Furthermore, since \(H\cap K\) is a subgroup of both \(H\) and \(K\) we have by Lagrange's Theorem that \(|H\cap K|\mid \operatorname{gcd}(q,p)=1\text{,}\) so that \(H\cap K=\{e_G\}\text{.}\) From here it follows that
    \begin{equation*} |HK|=\frac{|H||K|}{|H\cap K|}=\frac{q\cdot p}{1}=pq=|G| \end{equation*}
    and so \(HK=G\text{.}\) The recognition theorem now yields that
    \begin{equation*} G \cong H \rtimes_\rho K \cong C_q\rtimes_\rho C_p. \end{equation*}
    for some homomorphism \(\rho: K \to \operatorname{Aut}(H)\text{,}\) equivalently \(\rho: C_p \to \operatorname{Aut}(C_q)\text{.}\) By the UNM of cyclic groups to give such a homomorphism \(\rho\) is equivalent to giving an element \(z\in \operatorname{Aut}(C_q)\) so that \(z^p=\text{id}\text{,}\) which will give \(\rho(y^j)=z^j\) for \(C_p=\langle y\rangle\text{.}\) Thus \(|z|\mid p\) yielding that either \(|z|=1\) or \(|z|=p\text{.}\)
    Case 1: if \(|z|=1\) then \(\rho\) is the trivial homomorphism and thus \(G\cong C_q\times C_p\cong C_{pq}\text{.}\)
    Case 2: if \(|z|=p\) then it must be the case by Lagrange that \(p\mid |\operatorname{Aut}(C_q)\text{.}\) By Lemma Corollary 3.49 we know that \(\operatorname{Aut}(C_q)\cong C_{p-1}\) is a cyclic group. Therefore we have that \(p\mid (q-1)\) if and only if there exists an element \(z\in \operatorname{Aut}(C_q)\) of order \(p\) by [provisional cross-reference: thm:cyclic] (2) . Moreover any such element \(z\) generates a subgroup of \(\operatorname{Aut}(C_q)\) of order \(p\text{.}\) Since there is a unique subgroup of a cyclic group of a given order by Theorem [provisional cross-reference: thm:cyclic] (2) we see that the image of \(\rho\) is independent of the choice of \(z\text{.}\) Thus by Proposition [provisional cross-reference: prop:conjugateAut] we conclude that all subgroups resulting from any choice of \(z\) of order \(p\) are isomorphic.
    Moreover, from the explicit presentation of semidirect products of cyclic groups given in a homework problem we see that the resulting group is non-abelian; in particular it is not isomorphic to \(C_{pq}\text{.}\)

    Subsection Groups of Small Order

    Example 8.11. Groups of Order \(30\).

    Let's classify all groups of order \(30 = 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 5\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Right off the bat we see that there are at least four:
    \begin{equation*} \mathbb{Z}/30, D_{30}, \mathbb{Z}/5 \times D_6, \mathbb{Z}/3 \times D_{10}. \end{equation*}
    No two of these are isomorphic, as can be seen by looking at the sizes of their centers. The first is abelian, and we have \(\# Z(D_{30}) = 2\text{,}\) \(\#Z(\mathbb{Z}/5 \times D_6) = 5 \cdot 2 = 10\) and \(\#Z(\mathbb{Z}/3 \times D_{10}) = 3 \cdot 2 = 6\text{.}\)
    Let's show this list is complete. If \(G\) is a group of order \(30\text{,}\) then \(\# \operatorname{Syl}_2(G) \in \{1, 3, 5, 15\}\text{,}\) \(\# \operatorname{Syl}_3(G) \in \{1, 10\}\text{,}\) \(\# \operatorname{Syl}_5(G) \in \{1, 6\}\text{.}\) The usual counting argument shows that \(\# \operatorname{Syl}_3(G) = 10\) and \(\# \operatorname{Syl}_5(G) = 6\) cannot both occur. So, letting \(P \in \operatorname{Syl}_3(G)\) and \(Q \in \operatorname{Syl}_5(G)\text{,}\) since at least one is normal, we have that \(N := PQ\) is a subgroup of \(G\) of order \(15\text{.}\) Since it has index two, \(N \unlhd G\text{.}\) Moreover, since \(\#N = 15\text{,}\) by Sylow Theory, \(N\) has normal subgroups of order \(3\) and \(5\text{,}\) from which it follows that \(N \cong \mathbb{Z}/3 \times \mathbb{Z}/5 \cong \mathbb{Z}/15\text{.}\) Let \(H\) be a Sylow \(2\)-subgroup. Then
    \begin{equation*} G \cong N \rtimes_\rho H \end{equation*}
    for some homomorphism
    \begin{equation*} \rho: H \to \operatorname{Aut}(N), \end{equation*}
    where
    \begin{equation*} H \cong \mathbb{Z}/2 { \text{ and } }N \cong \mathbb{Z}/3 \times \mathbb{Z}/5 \cong \mathbb{Z}/15. \end{equation*}
    SInce \(H \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\text{,}\) each non-trivial map \(\rho\) is given by an element of order \(2\) in \(\operatorname{Aut}(N)\text{.}\) Now \(\# \operatorname{Aut}(N) = \phi(15) = 8\text{,}\) but this is not enough information to figure out how many elements have order \(2\text{.}\) We need to find \(\operatorname{Aut}(N)\) up to isomorphism.
    There is an evident homomorphism
    \begin{equation*} \theta: \operatorname{Aut}(\mathbb{Z}/3) \times \operatorname{Aut}(\mathbb{Z}/5) \to \operatorname{Aut}(\mathbb{Z}/3 \times \mathbb{Z}/5) \end{equation*}
    given by sending \((\alpha, \beta)\) to the function \((i,j) \mapsto (\alpha(i), \beta(j))\text{.}\) It's easy to see \(\theta\) is one-to-one and hence bijective by the pigeon-hole principle. It follows that
    \begin{equation*} \operatorname{Aut}(N) \cong (\mathbb{Z}/3)^\times \times (\mathbb{Z}/5)^\times \cong \mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/4. \end{equation*}
    In particuar, \(\operatorname{Aut}(N)\) has exactly \(3\) elements of order two, corresponding to \((1,0), (0,2), (1,2)\) in \(\mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/4\text{.}\) (One could also use that \(\operatorname{Aut}(N) \cong (\mathbb{Z}/15)^{\times}\) and then hunt around for elements of order \(2\text{.}\)) This gives exactly four choices for \(\rho\text{,}\) including the trivial map, and hence there are at most four groups of order \(30\text{.}\)

    Example 8.12. Groups of Order \(12\).

    Let's classify all groups of order \(12\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    As we will show below, every such group is a semi-direct product. Let's start by building all the groups we can as semi-direct products. As a matter of noation, let's write \(C_n\) for a cyclic group of order \(n\text{.}\) Let's consider the four possibilities:
    1. \(C_4 \rtimes_\rho C_3\text{,}\)
    2. \((C_2 \times C_2) \rtimes_\rho C_3\text{,}\)
    3. \(C_3 \rtimes_\rho C_4\text{,}\)
    4. \(C_3 \rtimes_\rho (C_2 \times C_2)\text{.}\)
    In case (1), since \(\# \operatorname{Aut}(C_4) = 2\text{,}\) we have only the abelian group \(C_4 \times C_3\text{.}\)
    In case (2), \(\# \operatorname{Aut}(C_2 \times C_2) = \# \operatorname{GL}_2(\mathbb{F}_2) = 6\) and so there is a unique subgroup of order \(3\text{.}\) These leads to three possibities for \(\rho\text{,}\) including the trivial one. The two non-trivial maps have conjugate (in fact, equal) images. By the homework problem, we get just two possible groups: one is the abelian group \(C_2 \times C_2 \times C_3\) and the other is non-abelian.
    In case (3), \(\# \operatorname{Aut}(C_3) = 2\) and so there are two possibilities for \(\rho\) including the trivial map. These leads to \(C_3 \times C_4\) and a non-abelian group.
    Case (4) is more complicated. We have \(\operatorname{Aut}(C_3)\) is itself cyclic of order \(2\text{.}\) Switching to additive notation, the map
    \begin{equation*} \rho: C_2 \times C_2 \to \operatorname{Aut}(C_3) \end{equation*}
    is given as a map
    \begin{equation*} \rho: \mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2 \to \mathbb{Z}/2, \end{equation*}
    of elementary abelian \(2\)-groups. Thus, \(\rho\) may be represented by a \(1 \times 2\) matrix with entries in \(\mathbb{F}_2\text{;}\) there are \(4\) such maps. But, using linear algebra, if \(\rho_1, \rho_2\) are any two non-trivial maps of this form, then there is an automorhism \(\beta\) of \(\mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2\) such that \(\rho_2 = \rho_1 \circ \beta\text{.}\) Using the Lemma below, this proves that
    \begin{equation*} (\mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2) \rtimes_{\rho_1} \mathbb{Z}/2 \cong (\mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2) \rtimes_{\rho_2} \mathbb{Z}/2 \end{equation*}
    for any two non-trivial map. Thus, in case (4) we again get just two groups up to isomorphism: the abelian group \(C_3 \times C_2 \times C_2\) and a non-abelian one.
    We must now beware to toss out reptitions: we've counted each abelian group twice. So, in total we've found at most five groups of order \(12\text{:}\)
    1. \(C_4 \times C_3\text{,}\)
    2. \(C_2 \times C_2 \times C_3\text{,}\)
    3. \((C_2 \times C_2) \rtimes_{\rho_1} C_3\text{,}\)
    4. \(C_3 \rtimes_{\rho_2} C_4\text{,}\)
    5. \(C_3 \rtimes_{\rho_3} (C_2 \times C_2)\text{,}\)
    where each of \(\rho_1, \rho_2, \rho_3\) is non-trivial.
    To see that no two of these are isomorphic, note that (a) and (b) are abelian and (c), (d), and (e) are not. Every Sylow \(2\)-subgroup of (d) is \(C_4\) whereas the Sylow \(2\)-subgroups for (c) and (e) are \(C_2 \times C_2\text{.}\) Finally, (c) and (e) are not isomorphic since the former has a normal Sylow \(2\)-subgroup and the latter does not.
    Finally, we need to prove we've found all groups of order \(12\text{.}\) If \(\# G = 12\text{,}\) then \(\operatorname{Syl}_2(G) \in \{1, 3\}\) and \(\operatorname{Syl}_3(G) \in \{1, 4\}\text{.}\) \(\operatorname{Syl}_2(G) = 3\) and \(\operatorname{Syl}_3(G) = 4\) cannot both occur since that would lead to at least \(8 + 6\) elements. This gives the existence of \(N \unlhd G\) and \(H \leq G\) with either \(\# N = 4, \# H = 3\) or \(\# N = 3, \# H = 4\text{.}\) The usual reasoning gives \(H \cap N = \{e\}\) and \(HN = G\text{,}\) so that \(G \cong N \rtimes_\rho H\) for some \(\rho: H \to \operatorname{Aut}(N)\text{.}\)

    Exercises Qualifying Exam Problems

    1. May 2018 (3).

    Determine all of the groups of order \(45\text{,}\) up to isomorphism.
    Solution.
    By Sylow's Theorem we know the following: - \(n_3|5\) and \(n_3\equiv 1\mod{3}\text{,}\) so \(n_3=1\text{.}\) - \(n_5|\) and \(n_5\equiv1\mod{5}\text{,}\) so \(n_5=1\) as well. Thus there is exactly one Sylow \(5\)-subgroup, \(P\text{,}\) and exactly one Sylow \(3\)-subgroup, \(Q\text{.}\) Both are normal in \(G\text{.}\) Notice that \(Q\) has order \(9\text{,}\) a prime squared. Thus \(Q\) is abelian. By the FTFGAG, \(Q\) is either isomorphic to \(\Z_3\times\Z_3\) or \(\Z_9\text{.}\) Thus \(G\cong\Z_5\times\Z_9\) or \(G\cong\Z_5\times\Z_3\times\Z_3\text{.}\)

    2. January 2017 (3).

    Let \(p\) be any positive prime integer. Prove that the number of groups of order \(3\cdot p\text{,}\) up to isomorphism, is exactly
    \begin{equation*} \begin{cases} 2, \text{ when }p = 2, p = 3, \text{ or }p\equiv 1 \mod 3, \text{ and }\\ 1, \text{ otherwise }\end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Let \(p\) be any positive prime integer.
    First, suppose \(p=2\text{.}\) Thus \(G\) is a group of order \(pq\text{,}\) making it abelian. So the only groups of order \(6\) are \(\Z_2\times\Z_3\) and \(\Z_6\text{.}\) The same applies when \(p=2\text{,}\) where the groups are \(\Z_3\times\Z_3\) and \(\Z_9\text{.}\)
    Let \(P\) be a Sylow \(p\)-subgroup of \(G\text{,}\) and note that \([G:P]=3\text{,}\) the smallest prime dividing the order of \(G\text{,}\) making \(P\nsg G\text{.}\) Let \(Q\) denote a Sylow \(3\)-subgroup of \(G\text{.}\) As \(P\) and \(Q\) are groups of relatively prime order we have \(PQ=G\) and thus \(G\cong P\sdp_\rho Q\text{,}\) where \(\rho:Q\to\Aut(P)\text{.}\) Notice that since \(|P|=p\text{,}\) we have \(\Aut(P)\cong\Z^{\times}_p\cong\Z_{p-1}\text{.}\) Thus, by the First Isomorphism Theorem \(Q/\ker(\rho)\cong\im(\rho)\leq\Z_{p-1}.\) As \(Q\) has three elements, the kernel of \(\rho\) must be either all of \(Q\) or trivial. However, the order of the image must divide \(\Z_{p-1}\text{,}\) which is only possible when \(p\equiv 1 \mod 3\text{.}\) Thus when this is the case there are two groups of order \(3p\text{,}\) otherwise the kernel is always trivial and we have \(G=P\times Q\) as the only group.

    3. January 2021 (3).

    Let \(G\) be a group of order \(175 = 3^2\cdot 5^2\) and suppose \(G\) contains an element of order \(25\text{.}\) Prove that \(G\) is abelian.
    Solution.
    First, note that \(3^2\cdot 5^2=225\text{,}\) not \(175\text{.}\) You hate to see it. Anyway, let \(x\) be an element of order \(25\) and consider \(H=\langle x\rangle\text{,}\) a cyclic subgroup of order \(25\text{.}\) The possible number of Sylow \(5\)-subgroups of \(G\) is exactly \(1\text{,}\) making \(H\) this subgroup and thus normal in \(G\text{.}\) Let \(K\) be a Sylow \(3\) subgroup, it intersects \(H\) trivially and thus \(G\cong H\sdp_{\rho}K\text{,}\) where \(\rho:K\to\Aut(H)\text{.}\) The order of \(\Aut(H)\) is \(20\text{,}\) which is relatively prime to \(|K|\text{,}\) making \(\rho\) trivial and \(G=H\times K\) and thus abelian.

    4. May 2019 (1).

    Let \(G\) be a group. A subgroup \(H\) of \(G\) is called maximal if \(H\neq G\) (that is, \(H\) is a proper subgroup of \(G\)) and whenever \(K\) is another subgroup of \(G\) containing \(H\text{,}\) either \(K = H\) or \(K = G\text{.}\) Show that every nontrivial finitely generated group \(G\) possesses maximal subgroups.
    Solution.
    Let \(G\) be a group.
    Let \(S\) be the poset of all proper subgroups of \(G\) ordered in terms of inclusion. Consider a string of these. Consider the union of them all. Luckily, unions of subgroups are subgroups if and only if there is containment, which there is, since everything is in the union. Thus its a subgroup. Since union in \(S\) and yields an upper bound, by Zorn's Lemma we a maximal element. Thus \(G\) possesses maximal subgroups.
