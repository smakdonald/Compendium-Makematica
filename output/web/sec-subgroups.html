    “I have this fear of falling in front of large groups of people. That's why I tend not to wear heels.”
    ―Taylor Swift

    Subsection Subgroup Basics

    Definition 1.45. Subgroup.

    A nonempty subset \(H\) of a group \(G\) is called a subgroup, denoted \(H\leq G\text{,}\) provided \(H\) is a group under the multiplication law of \(G\text{.}\)
    A subgroup \(H\) of a group \(G\) is a proper subgroup, denoted \(H<G\) if \(H\neq G\text{.}\)

    Lemma 1.46. Subgroup Tests.

    1. If a subset \(H\) of a group \(G\) is nonempty and closed under multiplication and inversion, then \(H\) is a subgroup.
    2. If a subset \(H\) of a group \(G\) is nonempty and satisfies for all \(x,y \in H\text{,}\) \(xy^{-1} \in H\text{,}\) then \(H\) is a subgroup.

    Proof.

    We prove the one-step test first.
    Assume \(H\) is non-empty and for all \(x,y \in H\text{,}\) \(xy^{-1} \in H\text{.}\) Since \(H\) is non-empty, there is an \(h \in H\) and hence \(e_G = hh^{-1} \in H\text{.}\) Since \(e_Gx=x=xe_G\) for any \(x\in H\text{,}\) \(e_G\) is an identity element for \(H\text{.}\) For any \(h \in H\text{,}\) \(h^{-1} = eh^{-1} \in H\text{,}\) and so every element of \(H\) has an inverse inside \(H\text{.}\) For \(x,y \in H\) we have \(y^{-1} \in H\) and thus \(xy = x(y^{-1})^{-1} \in H\) and hence \(H\) is closed under \(\cdot\text{.}\) This means that the restriction of the group operation of \(G\) to \(H\) is a well-defined group operation. This operation is associative by the axioms for the group \(G\text{.}\) The axioms of a group have now been established for \((H, \cdot)\text{.}\)
    Now we prove the two step test.
    Assume \(H\) is non-empty and closed under multiplication and inversion. Then, for \(x,y\in H\) we have \(y^{-1}\in H\) and \(xy^{-1}\in H\text{.}\) Since the hypothesis of the one-step test is satisfied, \(H\) is a subgroup of \(G\text{.}\)

    Example 1.47. Common Subgroups.

    1. \(\{e_G\}\) and \(G\) are the trivial subgroups of \(G\text{.}\)
    2. \(\displaystyle \Z\leq\Q\leq\R\leq\C\)
    3. \(\displaystyle \Q^\times\leq\R^\times\leq\C^\times\)
    4. Let \(n\in\N\) and define \(n\Z=\{x\in\Z|x=nk\;\text{for some}\;k\in\Z\}\text{.}\) Then \(n\Z\leq\Z\) for all \(n\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.48. Properties of Subgroups.

    1. Transitivity of Subgroups.
      If \(H\) is a subgroup of \(G\) and \(K\) is a subgroup of \(H\text{,}\) then \(K\) is a subgroup of \(G\text{.}\)
    2. Intersections of Subgroups.
      If \(H_\alpha\) is a subgroup of \(G\) for all \(\alpha\) in an index set \(J\text{,}\) then \(H=\bigcap_{\alpha\in J} H_\alpha\) is a subgroup of \(G\text{.}\)
    3. Unions of Subgroups.
      Let \(G\) be a group and \(H\text{,}\) \(H'\) subgroups. Prove that \(H\cup H'\) is a subgroup if and only if \(H\subseteq H'\) or \(H'\subseteq H\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.49. HK.

    Let \(H\) and \(K\) be subgroups of a group and define the set
    \begin{equation*} HK = \{hk \mid h \in H, k\in K\}. \end{equation*}

    Proposition 1.50.

    Let \(G\) be a group with subgroups \(H, K\text{.}\) Then the set
    \begin{equation*} HK=\{hk|h\in H,k\in K\} \end{equation*}
    is a subgroup of \(G\) if and only if \(HK=KH\text{.}\)

    Proposition 1.51. Subgroups of Abelian and Cyclic Groups.

    Let \(H\) be a subgroup of a group \(G\text{.}\) If \(G\) is abelian then so is \(H\text{.}\)

    Lemma 1.52.

    Let \(H\) and \(G\) be groups. If \(H\sse G\text{,}\) then \(H\leq G\)

    Definition 1.53. Alternating Group.

    The alternating group \(A_n\) is the subset of all even cycles of \(S_n\text{.}\)

    Proposition 1.54. \(A_n\leq S_n\).

    Prove that \(A_n\) is a subgroup of \(S_n\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.55. Special Linear Group.

    The special linear group with complex entries is defined
    \begin{equation*} \SL_n(\C)=\{A \mid A = n\times n \;\text{matrix with entries in}\; \C, \det(A)=1\} \end{equation*}

    Proposition 1.56.

    Prove that \(SL_n(\C)\) is a subgroup of \(GL_n(\C)\text{.}\)

    Subsection The Center of a Group

    Definition 1.57. Center.

    The center of a group \(G\text{,}\) often written \(Z(G)\text{,}\) is the set of elements of \(G\) that commute with every element of \(G\text{.}\) That is,
    \begin{equation*} Z(G)=\{x \in G \, | \, xy = yx\,\text{ for all $y \in G$}\}. \end{equation*}

    Proposition 1.58. Center is a Subgroup.

    Prove that \(Z(G)\) is an abelian subgroup for any group \(G\text{.}\)

    Proposition 1.59.

    Let \(G\) be a group. Prove \(G\) is abelian if any only if \(G=Z(G)\text{.}\)

    Proposition 1.60.

    Let \(H\leq G\) be a subgroup. Prove \(Z(H)\leq Z(G)\text{.}\)

    Subsection Generated Subgroups

    Definition 1.61. Generated Subgroup.

    Given a group \(G\) and a subset \(X\) of \(G\text{,}\) the subgroup of \(G\) generated by \(X\) is
    \begin{equation*} \langle X \rangle := \bigcap_{H \leq G, H \supseteq X} H. \end{equation*}
    If \(X=\{x\}\) is a set with one element then we write \(\langle X \rangle=\langle x \rangle\) and we refer to this as the cyclic subgroup generated by \(x\text{.}\)

    Example 1.62. Commutator Subgroup.

    Let \(G\) be a group, \(S=\{aba\inv b\inv|a,b\in G\}\text{,}\) and \(G'=\igen S\text{.}\) This is known as the commutator subgroup of \(G\text{.}\)

    Remark 1.63.

    Commutator subgroups prove invaluable when it comes to something called abelianization, which is, loosely speaking, a way of "modding out" the non-commutative part of \(G\) to obtain a new group that is abelian.

    Lemma 1.64.

    For a subset \(X\) of \(G\text{,}\) the elements of \(\langle X \rangle\) can be described as:
    \begin{equation*} \langle X \rangle = \left\{x_1^{j_1} \cdots x_m^{j_m} \mid m \geq 0, j_1, \dots, j_m \in \mathbb{Z}\text{ and }x_1, \dots, x_m \in X \right\}. \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    Let \(S= \left\{x_1^{j_1} \cdots x_m^{j_m} \mid m \geq 0, j_1, \dots, j_m \in \mathbb{Z}\text{ and }x_1, \dots, x_m \in X \right\}\text{.}\) Since \(\langle X \rangle\) is a subgroup that contains \(X\text{,}\) it is closed under products and inverses, and thus must contain all elements of \(S\text{.}\)
    For the opposite containment, we just need to show the set \(S\) really is a subgroup. We use the one step test:
    • \(S \ne \emptyset\) since we allow \(m = 0\) and declare the empty product to be \(e_G\text{.}\)
    • If \(x_1^{j_1} \cdots x_m^{j_m}\) and \(y_1^{i_1} \cdots y_n^{i_m}\) are in \(S\) then
      \begin{equation*} x_1^{j_1} \cdots x_m^{j_m}(y_1^{i_1} \cdots y_n^{i_m})^{-1}=x_1^{j_1} \cdots x_m^{j_m} y_n^{-i_m} \cdots y_1^{-i_1} \end{equation*}
      is also in \(S\text{.}\)
    Therefore \(S\leq G\) and \(X\subseteq S\) (by taking \(m=1\) and \(j_1=1\)) and by the minimality of \(\langle X \rangle\) we obtain \(\langle X \rangle\subseteq S\text{.}\)

    Corollary 1.65.

    \(\langle x\rangle=\{x^j \mid j\in \mathbb{Z}\}\text{.}\)

    Proposition 1.66.

    1. The set \(n\Z\) is a subgroup of \(\Z\) for all \(n\in\Z\text{.}\)
    2. \(\displaystyle n\Z=\igen n\)

    Proposition 1.67. Generating \(A_n\).

    Prove that \(A_n\) is the subgroup of \(S_n\) generated by all produts of \(2\)-cyclies (disjoint or not). In symbols,
    \begin{equation*} A_n=\langle (i \ j )(k \ l) \mid i,j,k,l \in \{1, \ldots, n\}\rangle. \end{equation*}
