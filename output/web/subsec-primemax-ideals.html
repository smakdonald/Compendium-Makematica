    Section 3.1 Prime and Maximal Ideals

    Subsection Prime Ideals

    “I have as much chance of becoming Prime Minister as of being decapitated by a frisbee or of finding Elvis.”
    ―Boris Johnson

    Definition 3.1.

    A prime ideal of a commutative ring \(R\) is a proper ideal \(P\) such that whenever \(xy \in P\) for \(x, y \in R\text{,}\) we have \(x \in P\) or \(y \in P\text{.}\)

    Example 3.2.

    • In \(\Z\text{,}\) the prime ideals are \((0)\) and the ideals generated by prime integers \(P=(p)\text{,}\) where \(p\) is a prime integer. The maximal ideals are the ideals generated by prime integers. In particular \((0)\) is prime but not maximal.
    • In \(\Z[i]\) the ideal \((13)\) is not prime, because \(13=(3+2i)(3-2i)\in(13)\text{,}\) but \(3+2i\not \in (13)\) and \(3-2i\not \in (13)\) (because if \(3\pm2i=13\a\) then \(N(3\pm2i)=N(13)N(\a)\) so \(13=13^2N(\a)\text{,}\) a contradiction).
    • In \(\Z[x]\) the ideal \((2,x)\) is maximal and prime (proof in the \(\ex\) given later), the ideals \((2)\) and \((x)\) are prime but not maximal.

    Proposition 3.3.

    An ideal \(P\) is prime if and only if \(R \setminus P\) is closed under multiplication.

    Theorem 3.4.

    Let \(R\) be a commutative ring with \(1\neq 0\text{,}\) and let \(I\) be an ideal of \(R\text{.}\) The ideal \(I\) is prime if and only if \(R/I\) is an integral domain.

    Proof.

    Suppose \(I\) is prime. If \((r + I)(r' + I) = 0 + I\text{,}\) then \(rr' \in I\) and hence either \(r \in I\) or \(r' \in I\text{,}\) so that either \(r + I = 0\) or \(r'+ I = 0\text{.}\) This proves \(R/I\) is a domain. Suppose \(R/I\) is a domain and that \(xy \in I\text{.}\) Then \((x + I)(y + I) = 0\) in \(R/I\) and hence either \(x+ I = 0\) or \(y + I = 0\text{.}\) It follows \(x \in I\) or \(y \in I\text{,}\) so that \(I\) is prime.

    Proposition 3.5.

    Let \(A\) be a nontrivial ring. Then \(A\) is an integral domain if and only if the ideal \((0)\) is prime.

    Proposition 3.6.

    If \(R\) is a domain, \(S\) is a ring and \(f:R\to S\) is a ring homomorphism, then \(\ker(f)\) is a prime ideal.

    Proposition 3.7.

    If \(f:A\to B\) is a ring homomorphism and \(P\) is a prime ideal in \(B\text{,}\) then \(f\inv(P)\) is prime in \(A\text{.}\)

    Subsection Maximal Ideals

    “It's just like, we know how to push ourselves to the max.”
    ―Young Dolph

    Definition 3.8.

    A maximal ideal of an arbitrary ring \(R\) is a proper ideal \(M\) such that the only ideals of \(R\) containing \(M\) are \(M\) and \(R\text{.}\)

    Proposition 3.9.

    Give an explicit example of a ring homomorphism \(f:A\to B\) and maximal ideal \(\fm\) of \(A\) such that \(f(\fm)\) is not maximal in \(B\text{.}\)

    Theorem 3.10.

    Let \(R\) be a commutative ring with \(1\neq 0\text{,}\) and let \(I\) be an ideal of \(R\text{.}\) The ideal \(I\) is maximal if and only if \(R/I\) is a field.

    Proof.

    The first assertion follows immediately from the Lattice Isomorphism Theorem and the fact that \(R/I\) is a field if and only if its only ideals are \(0\) and \(R/I\text{.}\)

    Corollary 3.11.

    Every maximal ideal is prime.

    Proof.

    If \(I\) is maximal, then \(R/I\) is a field, which in particular implies that \(R/I\) is a domain, so \(I\) is prime.

    Theorem 3.12. Zorn's Lemma.

    Let \(P\) be a non-empty family of sets. Suppose that for each chain \(C\) in \(P\text{,}\) the set \(\bigcup_{C_{i}\in C}C_{i}\) is in \(P\text{.}\) Then \(P\) has a maximal element.

    Theorem 3.13.

    If \(R\) is a ring with \(1\neq 0\) and \(I\) is a proper ideal of \(R\text{,}\) then there is a maximal ideal of \(R\) containing \(I\text{.}\) In particular every ring \(R\) contains a maximal ideal.

    Proof.

    Let \(\cC\) be the set of proper ideals of \(R\) that contain \(I\) and view \(\cC\) as a poset under containment. We will apply Zorn’s Lemma. Suppose \(\cT\) is a totally ordered subset of \(\cC\text{.}\) We need to show \(\cT\) has an upper bound in \(\cC\text{.}\) If \(\cT\) is empty, \(I\) is such a bound. Otherwise, let \(U = \bigcup_{L \in \cT} L\text{.}\)
    Since \(\cT\) is non-empty, we have \(I \subseteq U\) and so \(U\neq \emptyset\text{.}\)
    Given \(x,y \in U\text{,}\) then \(x \in L, y \in L'\) for some \(L, L' \in \cT\text{.}\) Since \(\cT\) is totally ordered, either \(L \subseteq L'\) or \(L' \subseteq L\text{,}\) and hence \(x + y \in L\) or \(x +y \in L'\text{.}\) Either way, \(x + y \in U\text{.}\)
    For \(x \in U\) and \(r \in R\text{,}\) we have \(x \in L\) for some \(L \in \cT\) and hence \(rx \in L \subseteq M\text{.}\)
    This proves \(U\) is an ideal that contains \(I\text{.}\) Since every \(L\in\cT\) is a proper ideal, \(L\cap R^\times =\emptyset\text{,}\) so \(U\cap R^\times = \bigcup_{L \in \cT} L\cap R^\times=\emptyset\) and hence \(U\) is a proper ideal, so \(U\in\cC\text{.}\) By Zorn’s Lemma, we conclude \(\cC\) has at least one maximal element \(M\text{.}\) This is a maximal ideal in the sense of definition since if \(J\) is an ideal of \(R\) and \(M\subseteq J\) then either \(J=R\) or, if \(J\) is proper, then \(J\in \cC\text{,}\) which yields \(J=M\) by using that \(M\) is a maximal element of \(\cC\text{.}\)
    The existence of a maximal ideal follows by applying the first part of the theorem for \(I=(0)\text{.}\)

    Proposition 3.14.

    Let \(R\) be a commutative ring, and set \(I = \{r \in R | rn = 0 \;\text{for some integer}\; n\}\text{.}\) Prove that following assertions.
    1. \(I\) is an ideal in \(R\text{.}\)
    2. If \(R/I\) is a field, then each element of \(R\) is either a unit or in \(I\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    1. Let \(x,y\in I\text{.}\) Thus there exists integers \(n,m\) such that \(xn=0=ym\text{.}\) Consider
      \begin{equation*} (xy)(nm)=(xn)(ym)=0. \end{equation*}
      Thus \(xy\in I\text{.}\) Additionally, \(x+y(mn)=xmn+ymn=0+0=0\text{,}\) and so \(x+y\in I\) as well.
      We now consider \(I\) as an additive group. Notice that \(0n=0\) and thus the identity is in there. Let \(x\in I.\) We examine \(x\inv\text{.}\) Since \(xn=0\) we have \((xn)\inv=0\) and so \(x\inv n\inv=0\text{.}\) So \(x\inv\in I\text{.}\) Perfect. We have ourselves a subring.
    2. Suppose now that \(R/I\) is a field. Luckily for us, this makes \(I\) a maximal ideal.
      Let \(r\in R\text{.}\) If \(r\in I\text{,}\) great. Suppose its not. Then in \(R/I\) \(r\) is a unit, since we’re in a field now. So \(ru\in I\) for some \(u\in R\text{.}\) Thus \(run=0\) for some \(n\in\Z\text{.}\) Since \(ru\in I\) and \(I\) a maximal and thus prime ideal, we have \(u\in I\) or \(r\in I\text{,}\) a contradiction.

    Proposition 3.15.

    1. Prove that a finite integral domain \(D\) must be a field.
    2. Prove that if \(R\) is a commutative ring and \(P\subseteq R\) is a prime ideal such that \(P\) has finite index as an additive subgroup of \(R\text{,}\) then \(P\) is a maximal ideal. Give an example to show that this implication may fail if the finite index assumption is dropped.

    Proof.

    Let \(D\) be a finite integral domain.
    1. Let \(x\in D\text{,}\) and consider the set \(\{x^n|n\in\N\}\text{.}\) As \(D\) is finite there must exist \(n<m\in\N\) such that \(x^n=x^m\text{.}\) In integral domains cancellation is allowed, and thus we can cancel an \(x^n\) from both sides, yielding \(e=x^{m-n}\text{,}\) and thus \(x\cdot x^{m-n-1}=e\text{,}\) making \(x\) a unit of \(D\text{.}\) Thus \(D\) is a field.
    2. Let \(R\) be a commutative ring and \(P\subseteq R\) a prime ideal such that \(P\) has finite index as an additive subgroup of \(R\text{.}\)
      Consider \(R/P\text{,}\) which is a domain since \(P\) is a prime ideal. As \([R:P]=n\) for some \(n\in\N\text{,}\) we know that \(|R/P|=n\text{,}\) making it a finite domain. Thus \(R/P\) is a field by Part (a), making \(P\) maximal by the same theorem as above.
      Let \(R=\Z\) and consider \(R[x]\text{,}\) in which \((x)\) is a prime ideal. However, \(R[x]/(x)=\Z\text{,}\) which does not have finite order, nor is it a field.

    Proposition 3.16.

    Let \(R\) be a commutative ring with identity, and assume \(1\neq0\text{.}\)
    1. Prove that every maximal ideal of \(R\) is a prime ideal.
    2. Assume \(R\) is a finite ring. Prove that every prime ideal is a maximal ideal.

    Proof.

    1. Let \(M\) be a maximal ideal of \(R\text{.}\) We show that \(R/M\) is a field By the Lattice Isomorphism Theorem we know there is a one-to-one correspondence between the ideals of \(R/M\) and the ideals of \(R\) containing \(M\text{.}\) However, as \(M\) is maximal, the only ideals of \(R\) containing \(M\) are \(R\) and \(M\) itself, meaning that \(R/M\) has exactly two ideals, making \(R/M\) a field.
      We show that since \(R/M\) is a domain, \(M\) must be prime. Suppose by way of contradiction that \(M\) is not prime, so exist elements \(x,y\in R\) such that \(xy\in M\) but neither \(x\) nor \(y\) are in \(M\text{.}\) Thus \(xM\cdot yM=xyM=M\text{,}\) making \(x\) and \(y\) zero-divisors in \(R/M\text{,}\) contradicting the fact that \(R/M\) is a field. Thus \(M\) is prime.
    2. Let \(R\) be a finite ring, and let \(P\) be a prime ideal in \(R\text{,}\) making \(R/P\) an integral domain by Theorem 3.4. We show \(R/P\) is a field. Let \(a\not\in P\text{,}\) and consider \(a^n\) for all \(n\in\N\text{.}\) As \(R/P\) is finite, there exists some \(k<m\in N\) such that \(a^k=a^m\text{.}\) Then \(a^{m-k}\cdot a^k=a^m=a^k\text{,}\) meaning that \(a^{m-k}=e\text{.}\) Thus \(a\) has inverse \(a^{m-k-1}\text{,}\) making \(a\) a unit. Thus \(R/P\) is a field, making \(P\) a maximal ideal by Theorem 3.10

    Proposition 3.17.

    Let \(R\) be a commutative ring in which every element \(x\) satisfies \(x^n = x\) for some \(n > 1\text{.}\) Show that every prime ideal in R is maximal.

    Proof.

    Let \(R\) be a commutative ring in which every element \(x\) satisfies \(x^n = x\) for some \(n > 1\text{.}\) Let \(P\) be a prime ideal in \(R\text{.}\)
    Let \(rP\in R/P\text{,}\) meaning \(r\not\in P\text{.}\) Then \((rP)^n=r^nP=rP\) for some \(n>1\text{.}\) As \(R/P\) is a domain  1  we can cancel an \(r\) to see that \(r^{n-1}P=P\text{,}\) and thus \(rP\cdot r^{n-2}P=P\text{,}\) making \(rP\) a unit in \(R/P\text{.}\) Thus \(R/P\) is a field, making \(P\) maximal in \(R\text{.}\)
