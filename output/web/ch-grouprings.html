    Chapter 24 Group Rings and Representations

    Section 24.1 Representations

    Definition 24.1.

    Let \(G\) be a group. A representation of \(G\) over a field \(K\) is a \(K\)-vector space \(V\) equipped with a group homomorphism \(\rho:G\to \operatorname{Aut}_K(V)\text{.}\) More generally, a representation of \(G\) over a ring \(R\) is an \(R\)-module \(V\) equipped with group homomorphism \(\rho:G\to \operatorname{Aut}_R(V)\text{.}\) We may also say that \(G\) acts linearly on \(V\text{.}\)

    Remark 24.2.

    We can think of this data in a number of different ways.
    1. Given a representation \((V,\rho)\text{,}\) the map
      \begin{equation*} xy matrix \end{equation*}
      satisfies the properties
      1. \(\displaystyle e \cdot v= v\)
      2. \(\displaystyle gh \cdot v = g \cdot (h \cdot v)\)
      3. \(\displaystyle g \cdot (v+w)=(g \cdot v) + (g \cdot w)\)
      4. \(g \cdot rv = r (g\cdot v)\text{,}\)
      In particular, the first two conditions say that \(G\) acts on \(V\) in the sense of group action on a set, and the last two say that the action of any element is by an \(R\)-linear map. Conversely, any such function \(\psi\) yields a representation \((V,\rho)\text{.}\)
    2. If \(V=R^n\) is free, then \(\operatorname{Aut}_R(V) \cong \operatorname{GL}_n(R)\text{,}\) where \(\operatorname{GL}_n(R)\) is the group of \(n\times n\) invertible matrices with entries in \(R\text{.}\) By a slight abuse of notation, we will say that a group homomorphism \(G\to \operatorname{GL}_n(V)\) is a representation of \(G\text{.}\)

    Example 24.3.

    1. For any group \(G\text{,}\) and any \(R\)-module \(V\text{,}\) there is the trivial representation \(\rho:G\to \operatorname{Aut}_R(V)\) where \(\rho(g)=1_V\) for all \(g\in G\text{.}\) In this action, every element acts trivially on \(M\text{.}\)
    2. Any representation on \(V = R\) is determined by specifying a group homomorphism \(\rho:G \to \operatorname{Aut}_R(R)\cong R^\times\text{.}\)
      For example, if \(G = C_n=\langle g\rangle\) (the multiplicative cyclic group of order \(n\)) and \(R = \mathbb{C}\text{,}\) there are \(n\) possible such homomorphisms, determined by \(\rho(g)=e^\frac{2\pi k i}{n}\) where \(0 \leq k \leq n-1\text{.}\)
      Another important example of a rank 1 representation is the sign representation of the symmetric group \(S_n\text{,}\) given by the group homomorphism which assigns to each permutation its sign, regarded as an element of the arbitrary ring \(R\text{.}\)
    3. The symmetric group \(S_n\) acts on a free \(R\)-module with basis \(b_1,\dots,b_n\) by permuting coordinates: \(\rho(\sigma)(b_i) = b_{\sigma(i)}\text{.}\) For a concrete example, \(S_3\) acts on \({\mathbb{R}}^3\text{,}\) where, for example \((132) \cdot (a_1,a_2,a_3) = (a_2,a_3,a_1)\text{.}\)
    4. Let \(G = D_{2n}\text{,}\) symmetries of the equilateral polygon on \(n\) vertices. Then \(G\) acts linearly on \(V={\mathbb{R}}^2\) by rotations and reflections. If \(G\) is generated by \(r\) (rotation by \(2\pi/n\)) and \(l\) (reflection about the \(y\)-axis), then the associated group homomorphism \(\rho: G \to\operatorname{GL}_2({\mathbb{R}})\) maps
      \begin{equation*} \rho(r)=\begin{bmatrix} \cos(2 \pi/n) & -\sin(2 \pi/n) \\ \sin(2 \pi/n) & \cos(2 \pi/n) \end{bmatrix} \qquad \rho(l)=\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    5. Let \(R=K\) be a field,\(V =K^2\text{,}\) and let \(G=(K,+)\text{.}\) We see that the assignment
      \begin{equation*} \rho:G\to \operatorname{GL}_2(K) \quad \rho(\lambda)=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ \lambda & 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
      is a representation. In particular, if \(K=\mathbb{F}_p\text{,}\) this is a representation of \(C_p\text{.}\)

    Definition 24.5.

    If \(\rho:G \to \operatorname{Aut}_R(V)\) is a representation, a submodule \(W\leq V\) is \(G\)-stable if \(\rho(g)(W)\subseteq W\) for all \(g\in G\text{.}\)

    Example 24.6.

    For \(G=S_n\) acting by permuting a basis as above, \(\{(\lambda,\dots,\lambda) \ | \ \lambda\in K\}\) and
    \begin{equation*} \{(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_n) \ | \ \lambda_1+\cdots+\lambda_n=0\} \end{equation*}
    are stable subspaces.

    Example 24.7.

    For \(G=(K,+)\) acting on \(K^2\) as above, \(\{(0,\lambda) \ | \ \lambda\in K\}\) is a stable subspace.

    Proposition 24.8.

    Fix a group \(G\) and a ring \(R\text{.}\) The collection of left \(R\)-linear representations of \(G\) and \(G\)-equivariant maps between them forms a category which we will denote \(\mathbf{Rep}_{R}(G)\text{.}\)

    Section 24.2 Group Rings and Modules

    Definition 24.9.

    For any ring \(R\) and group \(G\text{,}\) we define the group ring \(R[G]\) as follows: As a set, \(R[G]\) is the free left \(R\)-module with basis \(G\text{;}\) that is,
    \begin{equation*} R[G]=\left\{ \sum_g r_g g \mid r_g = 0_R \text{ for all by a finite number of }g's \right\}. \end{equation*}
    We define addition as module addition; that is,
    \begin{equation*} \left(\sum_g r_g g \right) + \left(\sum_h s_h h \right) = \sum_{f \in G} \left(r_f + s_f \right) f. \end{equation*}
    Multiplication is the unique pairing that obeys the distributive laws and is such that \(R\) is a subring, \(1_RG\) is a subgroup of \((R[G]^\times, \cdot)\text{,}\) and every element of \(R\) commutes with every element of \(G\text{.}\) In general, we have
    \begin{equation*} \left(\sum_g r_g g\right) \cdot \left(\sum_h s_h h \right) = \sum_{f \in G} \left(\sum_{\substack{(g,h) \in G \times G\\ gh= f}} r_gs_h\right) f. \end{equation*}
    where the inner sum is over pairs of group elements whose product is \(f\text{.}\)

    Remark 24.10.

    As a matter of notation, the element \(1_R g\) will be written as just \(g\) and the element \(r e_G\) as just \(r\text{,}\) so that we will regard \(G\) and \(R\) as subsets of \(R[G]\text{.}\) They overlap in the one element \(1_R e_G\) which will be written as just \(1\text{.}\)

    Remark 24.11.

    When \(R\) is commutative (in particular when \(R\) is a field), \(R[G]\) is an \(R\)-algebra called the group \(R\)-algebra of \(G\text{.}\)

    Problem 24.12.

    For any ring \(R\) and \(G = C_n\text{,}\) prove there is a ring isomorphism
    \begin{equation*} R[C_n] \cong R[x]/(x^n -1). \end{equation*}

    Proposition 24.13. Universal Mapping Property of Group Rings.

    Let \(R, A\) be rings and \(G\) a group. Given a ring homomorphism \(\iota: R \to A\) and a group homomorphism \(f: G \to (A^\times, \cdot)\text{,}\) such that for every \(r\in R, g\in G\) we have that \(\iota(r)\) and \(f(g)\) commute in \((A,\cdot)\text{,}\) there is a unique ring homomorphism \(\alpha: R[G] \to A\) such that \(\alpha|_R = \iota\) and \(\alpha|_G = f\text{.}\) Explicitly, \(\alpha\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} \alpha\left(\sum_g r_g g\right) = \sum_g \iota(r_g) f(g). \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    Most of this follows from noticing that \(R[G]\) is a coproduct. Indeed, we can vie \(R[G]\) as an internal direct sum \(R[G]=\bigoplus_{g\in G} Rg\) and hence it is the coproduct for the family \(\{Rg\}_{g\in G}\) where each \(Rg\cong R\text{.}\) For each \(g\in G\) set up an \(R\)-module homomorphism \(f_g: Rg\to A\) by mapping \(f_g(r_gg)=\iota(r_g)f(g)\text{.}\) Then the definition of coproduct gives a unique \(R\)-module homomorphism
    \begin{equation*} \alpha: R[G]=\bigoplus_{g\in G} Rg \to A \text{ such that } \alpha|_{Rg}=f_g. \end{equation*}
    From the way we defined the maps \(f_g\) we can deduce that \(\alpha|_R = \iota\) and \(\alpha|_G = f\) and
    \begin{equation*} \alpha\left(\sum_g r_g g\right) = \sum_g \iota(r_g) f(g). \end{equation*}
    It remains to check that this map is in fact a ring homomorphism, i.e. it preserves multiplication. This can be done using the formula for \(\alpha\) above and the fact that \(\iota(R)\) and \(f(G)\) commute in \(A\text{.}\)

    Remark 24.14.

    If we assumed that \(A\) is an \(R\)-algebra in the proposition above, then we would not need the commutativity condition as \(\iota(R)\) is in the center of \(A\) so it commutes with everything.

    Lemma 24.15.

    Let \(R\) be a ring, \(V\) a left \(R\)-module, and \(G\) a group. There is a bijection
    \begin{equation*} xy matrix \end{equation*}
    Moreover, if \(V\) and \(W\) are representations, then \(\psi:V\to W\) is \(G\)-equivariant if and only if it is \(R[G]\)-linear.

    Proof.

    Given an \(R[G]\)-module structure on \(V\text{,}\) for every \(g\in G\text{,}\) there is a map \(m_g: V\to V\) given by \(v\mapsto g\cdot v\text{.}\) We have \(m_g (rv) = g (rv) = rg (v) = r m_g(v)\text{,}\) so \(m_g\) is \(R\)-linear. Moreover, the map \(\rho:G\to \operatorname{End}_R(V)\) that sends \(g\mapsto m_g\) preserves multiplication and identity: \(\rho(gh)(v) = gh v = g(hv) = \rho(g) \rho(h) (v)\) and \(\rho(e)(v) = v\text{.}\) Thus, we obtain an \(R\)-linear representation \(\rho:G\to \operatorname{Aut}_R(V)\text{.}\)
    Conversely, recall that a module structure on an abelian group is equivalent to a ring homomorphism to its endomorphism ring over \(\mathbb{Z}\text{.}\) Given a representation \(\rho:G\to \operatorname{Aut}_R(V)\) by considering \(\operatorname{Aut}_R(V)\subseteq \operatorname{End}_\mathbb{Z}(V)\) we get a group homomorphism \(f\) to the unit subgroup of \(\operatorname{End}_\mathbb{Z}(V)\text{.}\) The action of \(R\) on \(V\) gives a ring homomorphism \(\iota: R \to \operatorname{End}_\mathbb{Z}(V)\text{.}\) For \(r\in R\) and \(g\in G\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} (f(g) \circ \iota(r))(v) = f(g)(rv) = \rho(g)(rv) = r \rho(g)(v) = (\iota(r) \circ f(g))(v) \end{equation*}
    for all \(v\in V\text{.}\) Thus, by the universal property, we get a well-defined ring homomorphism \(R[G] \to \operatorname{End}_\mathbb{Z}(V)\text{,}\) and hence an \(R[G]\)-module structure, which is easily seen to follow the formula above.
    We leave the final claim as an exercise.

    Remark 24.16.

    We can think of these bijections as yielding mutually inverse functors \(F:\mathbf{Rep}_{R}(G) \to R[G]-\mathbf{Mod}\) and \(F^{-1}:R[G]-\mathbf{Mod} \to \mathbf{Rep}_{R}(G)\text{.}\)
