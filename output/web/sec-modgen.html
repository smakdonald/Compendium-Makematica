    Section 13.2 Generated Modules

    Proposition 13.16. Finite Generation and Quotient Modules.

    Let \(R\) be a ring with \(0\neq 1\text{.}\) Prove that if \(M\) is an \(R\)-module and \(N\) is a submodule of \(M\) such that \(N\) and \(M/N\) are finitely generated, then \(M\) is finitely generated.

    Proposition 13.17. Every Cyclic \(R\)-module \(\cong\) to \(R/I\) for some \(I\).

    Every cyclic \(R\)-module is isomorphic to \(R/I\) for some left ideal \(I\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Say \(M\) is cyclic and \(m \in M\) is a generator of \(M\text{,}\) so that \(M = \{rm \mid r \in R\}\text{.}\) Define \(f: R \to M\) to be the unique \(R\)-map with \(f(1) = m\text{.}\) Here I am applying the UMP for bases, using that \(\{1\}\) is a basis of \(R\) as a left \(R\)-modules. More explicitly,
    \begin{equation*} f(r) = f(r\cdot 1) = r f(1) = rm \end{equation*}
    for all \(r \in R\text{.}\) Then \(f\) is onto, since \(m\) generates \(M\text{.}\) Its kernel is a left ideal \(I\) of \(R\text{,}\) since submodules of \(R\) are the same thing as left ideals. By the FIT, there is an isomorphism \(R/I \xra{\cong} M\) sending \(r + I\) to \(rm\text{.}\)

    Proposition 13.18.

    Let \(R\) be a ring. Recall that we proved in class that every cyclic \(R\)-module is isomorphic to \(R/I\) for some left ideal \(I\text{.}\) Prove the left ideal \(I\) occurring this statement is unique; that is, if a cyclic \(R\)-module is isomorphic to \(R/I\) and \(R/J\) for left ideals \(I\) and \(J\text{,}\) then \(I = J\text{.}\)

    Remark 13.19.

    More generally, the same argument shows that if \(M\) is a finitely generated \(R\) module, say generated by \(n\) elements, then \(M \cong R^n/N\) for some submodule \(N\) of \(R^n\text{.}\)

    Lemma 13.20. \(M/IM\) is an \(R/I\)-module.

    For a commutative ring \(R\text{,}\) module \(M\) and ideal \(I\text{,}\) the rules for addition in \(M/IM\) and scaling by \(R/I\) on \(M/IM\) introduced above make \(M/IM\) into an \(R/I\)-module.
    Moreover, given another \(R\)-module \(N\) and an \(R\)-map \(f: M \to N\text{,}\) the function \(\ov{f}: M/IM \to N/IN\) given by \(\ov{f}(m + IM) = f(m) + IN\) is a well-defined \(R/I\)-module homomorphism.
    Finally, if \(g: N \to P\) is yet another \(R\)-module homomorphism, then \(\ov{g} \circ \ov{f} = \ov{g \circ f}\text{,}\) and we also have \(\ov{\id_M} = \id_{M/IM}\) for any \(R\)-module \(M\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    I leave some of the details as an exercise, but I will check a few of the necessary things:
    We already showed that the rule for scaling is well defined, and we know from 817 that the rule for addition is well-defined and that \((M/IM, +)\) is an abelian group. To show \(M/IM\) is an \(R/I\)-module, there remain four axioms to verify. For instance,
    \begin{equation*} ((r+I)(s+I))(m +IM) = (rs + I)(m + IM) = (rsm + IM) = (r+I)(sm +IM) = (r+I)((s+I)(m+IM)), \end{equation*}
    which verifies one of them; the other proofs are similar.
    Next, let me verify that the function \(\ov{f}\) is a well-defined \(R/I\)-map: Let \(g: M \to N/IN\) be the composition of \(R\)-maps \(M \xra{f} N \to N/IN\) (the second one being the canonical one), so that \(g(m) = f(m) + IN\text{.}\) Since \(f(\sum_i a_i m_i) = \sum a_i f(m_i) \in IN\) for any \(a_i\)'s belonging to \(I\) and \(m_i\)'s belonging to \(M\text{,}\) we have \(IM \subseteq \ker(g)\text{.}\) By the UMP for quotient modules, there is an induced \(R\)-map \(\ov{f}: M/IM \to N/IN\) given by \(\ov{f}(m + IM) = f(m) + IN\text{.}\) The map \(\ov{f}\) is so far only known to be an \(R\)-map, but it is in fact an \(R/I\)-map since
    \begin{equation*} \ov{f}((r + I)(m +IM)) = \ov{f}(rm +IM) = f(rm) + IN = rf(m) + IN = (r+I)(f(m) + IN). \end{equation*}
    The final assertions are clear from the formula for \(\ov{f}\) for an \(R\)-map \(f\text{.}\)

    Remark 13.21.

    The Lemma shows that the rules \(M \mapsto M/IM\) and \(f \mapsto \ov{f}\) determine what is known as a functor from the category of \(R\)-modules to the category of \(R/I\)-modules.

    Example 13.22. Maximal Ideals and Vector Spaces.

    Suppose \(I\) is a maximal ideal of a commutative ring \(R\text{.}\) Then \(R/I\) is a field, and given an \(R\)-module \(M\text{,}\) \(M/IM\) is a module over the field \(R/I\text{;}\) i.e., it is a vector space over this field. Moreover, if \(f: M \to N\) is an \(R\)-map then \(\ov{f}: M/IM \to N/IN\) is an \(R/I\)-linear transformation.

    Proposition 13.23.

    Let \(R\) be a non-zero commutative ring and suppose \(p: R^m \to R^n\) is a surjective homomorphism of \(R\)-modules for some non-negative integers \(m\) and \(n\text{.}\)
    1. Prove that if \(R\) is a field, then \(m \geq n\text{.}\)
    2. Prove that if \(R\) is any non-zero commutative ring, then \(m \geq n\text{.}\)

    Proposition 13.24. Finitely Generated Modules and Submodules.

    Let \(R\) be a ring, let \(M\) be a left \(R\)-module and let \(N \subseteq M\) be a left \(R\)-submodule.
    1. Prove that if \(M\) is finitely generated (as an \(R\)-module), then so is \(M/N\text{.}\)
    2. Prove that if \(M/N\) and \(N\) are both finitely generated, then so is \(M\text{.}\)
    3. Prove the converse to the previous part is false, as follows: Let \(F\) be a field and \(R = F[x_1, x_2, \dots]\text{,}\) the ring of polynomials in the infinite list of variables \(x_1, x_2, \dots\text{.}\) (So, an element of \(R\) is a {} \(F\)-linear combination of monomials of the form \(x_1^{e_1} \cdots x_n^{e_n}\) for \(n \geq 0\) and \(e_i \geq 1\text{.}\) Note that each element of \(R\) involves only a finite number of variables, but there is no uniform bound on how many such variables can be involved in the elements of \(R\text{.}\)) You may assume without proof that \(R\) is a ring with the usual rules for adding and multiplying polynomials, which make sense since each element of \(R\) involves only a finite number of variables. Finally let \(I\) be the ideal of \(R\) generated by the variables \(x_1, x_2, \dots\text{.}\) Prove \(R\) is finitely generated as an \(R\)-module but the submodule \(I\) is not.

    Proposition 13.25. Split Surjection.

    Let \(R\) be a ring, \(M\) and \(N\) left \(R\)-modules, and \(p: M \to N\) an \(R\)-module homomorphism. Assume that \(p\) is surjective. We say \(p\) is a split surjection if there exists an \(R\)-module homomorphism \(j: N \to M\) such that \(p \circ j = id_N\) (i.e., \(p(j(n)) = n\) for all \(n \in N\)).
    1. Prove that if \(N\) is free, then every surjective \(R\)-module homomorphism of the form \(p: M \to N\) is a split surjection.
    2. By giving an explicit example with justification, show the statement in part (1) would become false if \(N\) were not assumed to be free.

    Proposition 13.26. Split Injection.

    Assume \(R\) is a ring, \(W\) an \(R\)-module, and \(i: V \to W\) an injective \(R\)-module homomorphism. We say \(i\) is a split injection if there exists an \(R\)-module homomorphism \(q: W \to V\) such that \(q \circ i = id_V\) (i.e., \(q(i(v)) = v\) for all \(v \in V\)).
    2. Prove that if \(R\) is a field, \(V\) is a subspace (i.e., submodule) of \(W\text{,}\) and \(i\) is the inclusion map, then \(i\) is a split injection. Note that since \(i\) is the inclusion map, what you need to prove is that there exists an \(R\)-module homomorphism \(q: W \to V\) such that \(q(v) = v\text{.}\) {}: Start by picking a basis \(B\) of \(V\) and use a theorem proven in class to show that \(B\) can be extended to a basis \(C\) of \(W\text{.}\) Use \(C\) to construct \(q\text{.}\) (I am assuming that \(V\) is a subspace of \(W\) and that \(i\) is the inclusion map just for simplicity — more generally, it is true that every injective \(R\)-module homomorphism is a split injection whenever \(R\) is a field.)
    3. Assume \(R\) is a non-zero integral domain, but that it is not a field. Prove there exists an \(R\)-module homomorphism that is an injection but {} a split injection. {}: Pick \(a \in R\) such that \(a \neq 0\) and \(a\) is not a unit, let \(I\) be the proper ideal \(I = R \cdot a\) generated by \(a\) and show the inclusion map \(i: I \to R\) is not a split injection.

    Proposition 13.27.

    Theorem 13.28. Properties of Torsion Submodules.

    Let \(R\) be a domain and let \(M\) be an \(R\)-module. The torsion submodule of \(M\) is
    \begin{equation*} \Tor(M) = \{m \in M \mid rm = 0 \text{ for some } r \in R \textrm{ with } r \neq 0 \}. \end{equation*}
    Elements of \(\Tor(M)\) are called the torsion elements of \(M\text{,}\) and the module \(M\) is called torsion if \(\Tor(M) = 0\text{.}\) You may take for granted that this is actually a submodule of \(M\) without proof.
    1. Show that if \(M\) and \(N\) are \(R\)-modules, then \(\Tor(M \oplus N) = \Tor(M) \oplus \Tor(N)\text{.}\)
    2. Show that if \(M \cong N\text{,}\) then \(\Tor(M) \cong \Tor(N)\text{.}\)
    3. Show that if \(M\) is a free \(R\)-module then \(\Tor(M)=0\text{.}\)
    4. Show that if \(I\) is an ideal of \(R\) that is not principal, then \(I\) is a torsion-free \(R\)-module that is not a free \(R\)-module.
    5. Show that if \(I\neq(0)\) is an ideal of \(R\) then \(\Tor(R/I)=R/I\text{.}\)
    6. Suppose that R is a PID, and that \(M\) is a finitely generated \(R\)-module. Show that \(M\) is a torsion-free \(R\)-module if and only if \(M\) is a free \(R\)-module.

    3. June 2010 Problem (5).
    Let \(R\) be a (not-necessarily commutative) ring let \(M\) be a left \(R\)-module. The annihilator of \(M\) in \(R\) is defined to be
    \begin{equation*} \ann_R(M) = \{ r \in R \mid rm = 0 \text{ for all $m \in M$}\}. \end{equation*}
    1. Prove that \(\ann_R(M)\) is a \(2\)-sided ideal of \(R\text{.}\)
    2. Suppose \(M\) is an abelian group (i.e., a \(\Z\)-module) such that \(|M| = 400\) and \(\ann_\Z(M)\) is the ideal generated by \(20\text{.}\) How many possibilities, up to isomorphism, are there for \(M\text{?}\)
    Solution.
    1. Let \(a,b\in \ann_R(M)\text{.}\) Consider \((a+b)m=am+bm=0\text{.}\) Thus \(a+b\in \ann_R(M)\text{.}\) Let \(r\in\ann_R(M)\) and consider \(-r\text{.}\) Let \(m\in M\) and suppose \(-rm=n\) for some \(n\in R\text{.}\) Add \(rm\) to both sides to see that \(0=n+rm=n\text{.}\) Thus \(n=0\) and \(-rm=0\) for all \(m\in M\text{.}\) So \(-r\in\ann_R(M)\text{.}\)
      Note that as \(0m=0=m0\) for all \(m\in M\text{,}\) we know that \(0\in\ann_R(M)\text{.}\) As \(rm=0\) for all \(m\in M\text{,}\) we see that \(\ann_R(M)\) is a left sided ideal.
      Suppose \(mr=n\) for some \(m\in R\text{.}\) This time we add \(-rm\) to both sides, but as \(-r\in\ann_R(M)\text{,}\) we once again find that \(mr=0\text{.}\) Notice that this means \(rm=mr\) for all \(r\in \ann_R(M)\) and \(m\in M\text{,}\) and thus that elements of \(\ann_R(M)\) commute with elements of \(M\text{.}\)
      Let \(a,b\in \ann_R(M)\text{.}\) Consider \(m(a+b)=ma+mb\text{.}\) Luckily, we know \(ma=am=0=bm=mb,\) and thus that \(ma+mb=0+0=0\text{.}\) Hence \(a+b\in \ann_R(M)\text{,}\) making \(\ann_R(M)\) is a two sided ideal.
    2. By the FTFGAG and Sunzi's Remainder Theorem, there are only so many options we have for \(M\text{:}\)
      • \(\Z_{16}\times \Z_{25}\text{,}\)
      • \(\Z_{8}\times \Z_{2}\times \Z_{25}\text{,}\)
      • \(\Z_{4}\times \Z_{4}\times \Z_{25}\text{,}\)
      • \(\Z_{4}\times \Z_{2}\times \Z_{2}\times \Z_{25}\text{,}\)
      • \(\Z_{2}\times\Z_{2}\times \Z_{2}\times \Z_{2}\times \Z_{25}\text{,}\)
      • \(\Z_{16}\times \Z_{5}\times \Z_{5}\text{,}\)
      • \(\Z_{8}\times \Z_{2}\times \Z_{5}\times \Z_{5}\text{,}\)
      • \(\displaystyle \Z_{4}\times \Z_{5}\times \Z_{20}\)
      • \(\Z_{4}\times \Z_{2}\times \Z_{2}\times \Z_{5}\times \Z_{5}\text{,}\) and
      • \(\Z_{2}\times\Z_{2}\times \Z_{5}\times \Z_{20}\text{.}\)
      However, as ideals are additive subgroups, we know that \(M\) needs to contain a cyclic subgroup of order \(20\text{.}\) Thus we need only consider decompositions with a \(\Z_{20}\) in them, of which there are exactly two:
      1. \(\displaystyle \Z_{4}\times \Z_{5}\times \Z_{20}\)
      2. \(\displaystyle \Z_{2}\times\Z_{2}\times \Z_{5}\times \Z_{20}\)

    Solution.
    Let \(R^n=N\) and \(R^m=M\text{.}\)
    Let \(I\) be a maximal ideal in \(R\text{.}\) Thus \(R/I\) is a field. Lemma 1.58 tells us that \(M/IM\) and \(N/IN\) are \(R/I\)-vector spaces. Additionally, this gives rise to \(\overline{p}:M/IM\to N/IN\text{,}\) which is a surjective \(R/I\)-module linear transformation.

Likewise \(N/IN\) has a basis with \(n\) elements. As we are surjective, Rank\(=n\text{,}\) \(\dim=m\text{.}\) So by Rank-Nullity \(\dim(\ker)=m-n\) which is only positive with \(m\geq n\text{.}\)
    5.
    Recall that a \(\Z\)-module \(M\) is called torsion-free if its torsion submodule is \(\Tor(M ) =\{0\},\) where \(\Tor(M ) = \{m \in M | rm = 0 \text{ for some }r \in \Z\setminus\{0\}\}.\) Consider the \(\Z\)-module
    \begin{equation*} M= \frac{\Z \oplus \Z}{\langle(7, 11)\rangle }\text{ where }\langle(7, 11)\rangle = \{(7k, 11k) | k \in \Z\}. \end{equation*}
    Show that \(M\) is torsion free.
    Solution.
    Let \(m=(a,b)\in\Tor(M)\text{.}\) Thus \(rm=0\) for some nonzero \(r\in\Z\text{.}\) Then \((ra,rb)=0\text{,}\) and so \(ra=0\) and \(rb=0\text{.}\) Then there exists \(p,q\in\Z\) such that \(7p=ra\) and \(7q=rb\) or \(11p=ra\) and and \(11q=rb\text{.}\) Notice that in \(M\) we have \(7,11=0\text{,}\) and thus neither \(7\) nor \(11\) can divide \(r\text{.}\) Suppose \(7p=ra\) and \(7q=rb\text{.}\) As \(7\) cannot divide \(r\) we see that \(7\) divides both \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) placing them both \(\langle(7, 11)\rangle\text{.}\) Thus \(m=0\text{.}\) The same holds true if we use \(11\text{.}\) Thus \(m=0\) and \(\Tor(M)=\{0\}\text{.}\)
