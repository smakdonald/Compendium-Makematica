    Section 1.4 Group Homomorphisms

    Subsection Homomorphism? I Barely Know 'em!

    “In the end, the character of a civilization is encased in its structures.”
    ―Frank Gehry

    Definition 1.56. Group Homomorphism.

    If \(G\) and \(H\) are groups, a homomorphism from \(G\) is \(H\) is a function \(\varphi: G \to H\) such that \(\varphi(x \cdot_G y) = \varphi(x) \cdot_H \varphi(y)\text{,}\) where \(\cdot_G\) and \(\cdot_H\) denote the multiplication rules for \(G\) and \(H\text{,}\) respectively.
    We use \(\varphi\) to denote an arbitrary homomorphism instead of \(f\) to differentiate between homormorphisms and ordinary functions.
    Some folk use \(f\) for homomorphisms as well as functions that are not homomorphisms. They should be avoided and probably smell weird.
    Intuitively, a group homomorphism preserves the algebraic structure of the group, while allowing us to compare and relate different groups. In this way, group homomorphisms allow us to study the properties of groups by comparing them to other groups that we already understand well.

    Example 1.57. Homormophism Examples.

    1. The identity map is a group homomorphism for any group \(G\text{,}\) this is known as the trivial homomorphism.
    2. The zero map is a group homomorphism for any group \(G\text{.}\)
    3. The sign homomorphism \(\sign: S_n\to \{-1,1\}\) that sends even permuations to \(1\) and odd permutations to \(-1\) is a homomorphism.
    4. The exponential map \(\exp: ({\mathbb{R}}, +) \to (\mathbb{R}^\times, \cdot)\) is a homomorphism.
    5. The natural log map \(\ln: (\mathbb{R}^\times, \cdot) \to ({\mathbb{R}}, +)\) is a homomorphism.
    6. For any positive integer \(n\text{,}\) the map determinant map \(\det: \operatorname{GL}_n(\C) \to (\C^\times, \cdot)\) is a group homomorphism.
    Specifically, homomorphisms will preserve the additive structure, such as the identity element and inverse elements.

    Theorem 1.58. Homomorphism Preservations.

    If \(\varphi: G \to H\) is a homomorphism of groups, then
    1. \(\varphi(e_G) = e_H\) and
    2. \(\varphi(x^{-1}) = \varphi(x)^{-1}\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    For the first, \(\varphi(e_G) = \varphi(e_Ge_G) = \varphi(e_G)\varphi(e_G)\) and now multiply by \(\varphi(e_G)^{-1}\text{.}\) For the second, \(\varphi(x^{-1}) \varphi(x) = \varphi(e) = e\) implies \(\varphi(x^{-1}) = \varphi(x)^{-1}\text{.}\)

    Theorem 1.59. Compositions of Homomorphisms.

    If \(\varphi:G\to H\) and \(\phi:H\to K\) are group homomorphisms, the composition \(\phi\circ\varphi:G\to K\) is a group homomorphism.

    Exploration 1.1. \(\Hom\) Sweet \(\Hom\).

    Let \(G\) and \(H\) be abelian groups. Then the set \(\Hom(G,H)\) of all group homomorphisms from \(G\) to \(H\) is itself an abelian group.

    Definition 1.60. Kernel.

    Let \(\varphi: G \to H\) be a homomorphism of groups. The kernel of \(\varphi\) is the set
    \begin{equation*} \ker(\varphi) := \{g\in G \mid \varphi(g)=e_H\}. \end{equation*}

    Theorem 1.61. Injective and Surjective Homomorphisms.

    1. A group homomorphism \(\varphi: G \to H\) is injective if and only if \(\operatorname{ker}(\varphi) = \{e_G\}\text{.}\)
    2. A group homomorphism \(\varphi: G \to H\) is surjective if and only if \(\operatorname{im}(\varphi) = H\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    1. \(\Rightarrow\) is immediate from the definitions (since \(e_G \in \operatorname{ker}(f)\) for all homomorphisms \(f\)). If \(\operatorname{ker}(f) = \{e_G\}\) and \(f(h) = f(h')\) then \(f(h^{-1}h') = f(h)^{-1}f(h') = e_H\) and thus \(h^{-1}h' = e_G\) which implies \(h = h'\text{.}\)
    2. This is identical to Exercise A.17; the homomorphism aspect adds nothing of interest.  18

    Subsection Isomorphisms

    “The test of a first-rate intelligence is the ability to hold two opposed ideas in mind at the same time and still retain the ability to function.”
    ―F. Scott Fitzgerald

    Definition 1.63. Group Ismorphism.

    A homomorphism \(\varphi: G \to H\) is called an isomorphism if there exists a homomorphism \(\phi: H \to G\) such that \(\varphi \circ \phi = \operatorname{id}_H\) and \(\phi \circ \varphi = \operatorname{id}_G\text{.}\)
    If \(\varphi:G\to H\) is an isomorphism, \(G\) and \(H\) are called isomorphic, written \(G\cong H\text{.}\)
    Intuitively, a group isomorphism establishes a one-to-one correspondence between the elements of two groups, such that they have exactly the same algebraic structure. This means that the groups are essentially the same, up to a relabeling of their elements.

    Theorem 1.64. Equivalent Isomorphism Characterization.

    Suppose \(\varphi: G \to H\) is a group homomorphism. Then \(\varphi\) an isomorphism if and only if \(\varphi\) is bijective (one-to-one and onto).

    Proof.

    We start by recalling that a function \(\alpha: X \to Y\) between two sets is bijective if and only if there is a function \(\beta: Y \to X\) such that \(\alpha\circ \beta= \operatorname{id}_Y\) and \(\beta\circ \alpha= \operatorname{id}_X\text{.}\)
    The \(\Leftarrow\) is thus immediate. For the \(\Rightarrow\) direction, if \(\varphi\) is bijective homomorphism, then it certainly has a set-theoretic two-sided inverse \(\phi\text{.}\) But we need to show \(\phi\) is actually a homomorphism: for \(x,y \in H\) we have \(\varphi(\phi(xy)) = xy = (\phi(x))\varphi(g(y)) = \varphi(\phi(x)\phi(y))\text{.}\) Since \(\varphi\) is one-to-one, \(\phi(xy) = \phi(x)\phi(y)\text{.}\)

    Remark 1.65.

    Moving forward, we will essentially treat Theorem 1.64 as the de-facto definition of a group isomorphism. Rather than end every proof with a reference to Theorem 1.64, we will instead enshrine its monumental achievements of Theorem 1.64 within this remark. To alleviate some of the corresponding guilt asociated with this decision I am referencing Theorem 1.64 as many times as possible, as Theorem 1.64 is important and Theorem 1.64 should not be forgotten.

    Example 1.66. Isomorphism Examples.

    1. The identity map is a group isomomorphism for any group \(G\text{.}\)
    2. \((\Z,+)\cong(\E,+)\text{,}\) where \((\E,+)\) is as defined in Exercise 1.15.
    3. \(\displaystyle (\R,+)\cong(\R^\times,\cdot)\)
    4. The exponential and natural logarithm maps from Example 1.57 are inverses, making them isomorphisms as well.

    Theorem 1.68. Isomorphism Invariants.

    Let \(G\) and \(H\) be groups. If \(\varphi:G\to H\) is an isomorphism, then the following hold:
    1. \(\displaystyle |G|=|H|\)
    2. \(|g|=|\varphi(g)|\) for all \(g\in G\) and \(|h|=|\varphi\inv(h)|\) for all \(h\in H\)
    3. \(G\) is abelian if and only if \(H\) is abelian
    4. \(\displaystyle |Z(G)|=|Z(H)|\)
    5. \(G\) is finitely generated if and only if \(H\) is finitely generated.

    Definition 1.79. Inner Automorphism.

    Let \(G\) be a group and \(x\in G\text{.}\) The function \(\psi_x:G\to G\) defined by \(\psi_x(a)=xax^{-1}\) is called the inner automorphism of \(G\) induced by \(x\text{.}\) The set of inner automorphisms of \(G\) is denoted \(\Inn(G)=\{\psi_x\mid x\in G\}\text{.}\)
    Let's make sure this is indeed an automorphism to avoid any potential awkwardness down the line.

    Proposition 1.80. The Conjugation Automorphism.

    Let \(G\) be a group and \(x\in G\text{.}\) Then \(\psi_x\in \operatorname{Aut}(G)\) for all \(x\in G\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Notice \(\psi_g(xy) = gxyg^{-1} = gxg^{-1} gyg^{-1} = \psi_g(x) \psi_g(y)\text{,}\) proving that \(c_g\) is a homomorhism from \(G\) to itself.
    Observe that for any \(g, h \in G\text{,}\) we have \(\psi_g \circ \psi_h = \psi_{gh}\) since \(\psi_g(c_h(x)) = ghxh^{-1} g^{-1} = ghx(gh)^{-1} = \psi_{gh}(x)\text{.}\) Also, \(\psi_e = \operatorname{id}_G\text{,}\) which is obvious. It follows that \(\psi_g \circ \psi_{g^{-1}} = \operatorname{id}_G\) and \(\psi_{g^{-1}} \circ \psi_g = \operatorname{id}_G\text{.}\) This proves \(\psi_g\) has a two-sided inveerse and hence is an isomorphism. In fact, it shows that \((\psi_g)^{-1} = \psi_{g^{-1}}\text{.}\)

    There is indeed a notion of “outer automorphisms” as well, though we currently lack the tools to define them rigorously.
