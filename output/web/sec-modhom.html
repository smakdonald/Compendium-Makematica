    Section 13.2 Module Homomorphisms

    Subsection Homomorphisms and Isomorphisms

    “Transformation rarely happens accidentally.”
    ―A.J. Sheppard

    Definition 13.19. Module Homomorphism.

    Let \(R\) be a ring and let \(M\) and \(N\) be \(R\)-modules. An \(R\)-module homomorphism from \(M\) to \(N,\) sometimes called an \(R\)-map, is a function \(\varphi: M \to N\) such that for all \(r \in R\) and \(m,n \in M\) we have
    1. \(\varphi(m+n)=\varphi(m)+\varphi(n)\text{,}\) i.e. \(\varphi\) is an additive group homomorphism, and
    2. \(\varphi(rm) = r\varphi(m)\text{.}\)

    Exploration 13.20.

    \(\varphi(0)=0\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    The condition \(\varphi (m + n) = \varphi (m) + \varphi (n)\) says that \(\varphi\) is a homomorphism of abelian groups.

    Remark 13.21.

    Unlike its group or ring counterparts, a module homomorphism does not need to map \(1\) to \(1\text{.}\)
    When the ring of scalars is a field, special terminology is used:

    Definition 13.22. Linear Transformation.

    Let \(F\) be a field and let \(M\) and \(N\) be vector spaces over \(F\text{.}\) A linear transformation from \(M\) to \(N\) is an \(F\)-module homomorphism \(h:M \to N\text{.}\)

    Example 13.23.

    Let \(R\) be a commutative ring and \(M\) be an \(R\)-module. For each \(r ∈ R\text{,}\) the multiplication map \(μr : M → M\) given by \(μr(m) = rm\) is a homomorphism of \(R\)-modules.
    Solution.
    Indeed, by the definition of \(R\)-module we have
    \begin{equation*} μ_r(m + n) = r(m + n) = rm + rn = μ_r(m) + μ_r(n), \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} μ_r(sm) = r(sm) = (rs)m = (sr)m = s(rm) = sμ_r(m). \end{equation*}

    Definition 13.24. Module Isomorphism.

    An \(R\)-module homomorphism \(\varphi: M \to N\) is an \(R\)-module isomorphism if there is another \(R\)-module homomorphism \(\phi: N \to M\) such that \(\varphi \circ \phi = \id_M\) and \(\phi \circ \varphi = \id_N\text{.}\)
    To check that an \(R\)-module homomorphism \(\varphi : M → N\) is an isomorphism, it is sufficient to check that it is bijective.

    Theorem 13.25. Module Isomorphisms and Bijections.

    Given a ring \(R\text{,}\) \(R\)-modules \(M\) and \(N\text{,}\) and a \(R\)-module homomorphism \(\varphi: M \to N\text{,}\) \(\varphi\) is an \(R\)-module isomorphism if and only if \(\varphi\) is a bijection.
    One can think of a module isomorphism as a relabelling of the names of the elements of the module. If two modules are isomorphic, that means that they are essentially the same, up to renaming the elements.

    Definition 13.26. Kernel.

    The kernel of an \(R\)-module homomorphism is the set \(\ker(h) := \{m \in M \mid h(m) = 0_N\}\)

    Exploration 13.27.

    Let \(R\) be a ring with \(1 \ne 0\text{,}\) let \(M\) be an \(R\)-module, and let \(N\) be an \(R\)-submodule of \(M\text{.}\) Then the inclusion map \(i : N → M\) is an \(R\)-module homomorphism.

    Definition 13.28. Cokernel.

    The cokernel of an \(R\)-module homomorphism is the set

    Theorem 13.29. Kernels and Images are Submodules.

    Let \(R\) be a ring and let \(h: M \to M'\) be an \(R\)-module homomorphism. Then
    1. \(\ker(h) := \{m \in M \mid h(m) = 0_N\}\) is an \(R\)-submodule of \(M\) and
    2. \(\im(h)\) is an \(R\)-submodule of \(M'\text{.}\)

    Subsection \(\Hom\) and \(\End\)

    “All good things must come to an \(\End\)”
    ―Geoffrey Chaucer

    Definition 13.30. \(\Hom\) and \(\End\).

    Let \(R\) be a ring and let \(M\) and \(N\) be \(R\)-modules. Then \(\Hom_R(M, N )\) denotes the set of all \(R\)-module homomorphisms from \(M\) to \(N\text{,}\) and \(\End_R(M)\) denotes the set \(\Hom_R(M, M)\text{.}\) We call \(\End(M)\) the endomorphism ring of \(M\) , and elements of \(\End(M)\) are called endomorphisms of \(M\text{.}\)

    Exploration 13.31.

    1. \(\End(M)\) is a ring.
    2. \(HomR(M, N)\) is not a ring.

    Proposition 13.32.

    Let \(M\) and \(N\) be \(R\)-modules over a commutative ring \(R\text{.}\) Then \(\Hom_R(M, N )\) is an \(R\)-module using the following structure: Given \(\varphi, \phi \in \Hom_R(M, N )\text{,}\) \(\varphi + \phi\) is the map defined by \((\varphi + \phi)(m) := \varphi (m) + \phi(m)\text{,}\) and given \(r ∈ R\) and \(\varphi \in \Hom_R(M, N )\text{,}\) \(r \cdot \varphi\) is the \(R\)-module homomorphism defined by \((r \cdot \varphi )(m) := r \cdot \varphi (m) = \varphi (rm).\) The zero element of \(\Hom_R(M, N )\) is the zero map.

    Remark 13.33.

    We will see later that for an \(n\)-dimensional vector space \(V\) over a field \(F\text{,}\) there is an isomorphism of vector spaces \(EndF (V ) ∼= Mn(F )\text{.}\) This says that every linear transformation \(T : V → V\) corresponds to some \(n × n\) matrix. However, the story for general \(R\)-modules is a lot more complicated.

    Subsection Quotient Modules

    “The best way to solve a problem is to remove its cause.”
    ―Martin Luther King Jr.

    Definition 13.37. Quotient Module.

    Let \(R\) be a ring, let \(M\) be an \(R\)-module, and let \(N\) be a submodule of \(M\text{.}\) The quotient module \(M/N\) is the quotient group \(M/N\) under \(+\) (so elements of \(M/N\) are additive cosets of the form \(m + N\) with \(m \in M\) and addition is defined by \((m_1 + N) + (m_2 + N) = (m_1+m_2) + N\)) and with the rule for scaling by \(R\) defined to be
    \begin{equation*} r \cdot (m + N) = rm + N \end{equation*}
    for all \(r \in R\) and \(m + N \in M/N\text{.}\)

    Theorem 13.38. Quotient Modules are Well Defined.

    Let \(R\) be a ring, let \(M\) be an \(R\)-module, and let \(N\) be a submodule of \(M\text{.}\) The rule for scaling introduced above is well-defined and it, along with the rule for \(+\text{,}\) makes \(M/N\) into an \(R\)-module.

    Definition 13.39. Module Quotient Map.

    Let \(R\) be a ring, let \(M\) be an \(R\)-module, and let \(N\) be a submodule of \(M\text{.}\) Define the canonical quotient map \(\pi: M \to M/N\) by \(\pi(m) = m + N\text{.}\)

    Proposition 13.40. Quotient Map is an \(R\)-map.

    Let \(R\) be a ring, let \(M\) be an \(R\)-module, and let \(N\) be a submodule of \(M\text{.}\)
    1. The quotient map \(\pi: M \to M/N\) is a n \(R\)-module homomorphism
    2. \(\ker(\pi) = N\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Among the many things to check here, we will only check a couple.
    We need to prove the rule for scaling by \(R\) on \(M/N\) is well-defined: If \(m+N=m'+N\) then \(m-m'\in N\) so \(r(m-m')\in N\) by the definition of submodule. This gives that \(rm-rm'\in N\text{,}\) hence \(rm+N=rm'+N\text{.}\) The module axioms are then pretty straightforward. We already know from 817 that \(M/N\) is an abelian group under \(+\text{.}\)
    Let us check one of the four axioms involving scaling. We have
    \begin{equation*} r( (m_1 +N) + (m_2 +N)) = r((m_1 + m_2) + N) = (r(m_1+m_2)) + N = (rm_1 + rm_2) + N = (rm_1 + N) + (rm_2 + N), \end{equation*}
    which gives the third such axiom. The other three are also straightforward.
    The fact that \(\pi\) is an \(R\)-module homomorphism is also straightforward. Its kernel is \(\{m \in M \mid m + N = N\}\text{,}\) which is equal to \(N\text{.}\)

    Example 13.41. \(\Z\)-modules and Quotients.

    Recall that \(\Z\)-modules are the same as abelian groups by Theorem 13.9. Submodules and quotient \(\Z\)-modules are the same things as subgroups and quotients of abelian groups.

    Theorem 13.42. Module Isomorphism Theorems.

    Let \(R\) be a ring, and let \(M\) be a \(R\)-module.
    1. UMP for Quotient Modules.
      Let \(N\) be a submodule of \(M\text{,}\) let \(T\) be an \(R\)-module, and let \(f: M \to T\) be an \(R\)-module homomorphism. If \(f(M) = 0\) (i.e., if \(N \subseteq \ker(f)\)) then the function \(\ov{f}: M/N \to T\) given by \(\ov{f}(m + N) = f(m)\) is a well-defined, \(R\)-module homomorphism. In fact, \(\ov{f}: M/N \to T\) is the unique \(R\)-module homomorphism such that \(\ov{f}\circ \can = f\) where \(\can\) denotes the canonical surjection \(M \onto M/N\text{.}\)
    2. First Isomorphism Theorem.
      Let \(T\) be an \(R\)-module and let \(h: M \to T\) be an \(R\)-module homomorphism. Then \(\ker(h)\) is a submodule of \(M\) and there is an \(R\)-module isomorphism \(M/\ker(h) \cong \im(h)\) given by \(m + \ker(h) \mapsto h(m)\text{.}\)
    3. Second Isomorphism Theorem.
      Let \(A\) and \(B\) be submodules of \(M\text{,}\) and define \(A + B = \{a+b \mid a \in A, b \in B\}\text{.}\) Then \(A + B\) is a submodule of \(M\text{,}\) \(A \cap B\) is a submodule of \(A\text{,}\) and there is an \(R\)-module isomorphism \((A + B)/B \cong A/(A \cap B)\text{.}\)
    4. Third Isomorphism Theorem.
      Let \(A\) and \(B\) be submodules of \(M\) with \(A \subseteq B\text{.}\) Then \(B/A\) is a submodule of \(M/A\) and there is an \(R\)-module isomorphism \((M/A)/(B/A) \cong M/B\) given by sending \((m + A) + B/A\) to \(m + B\text{.}\)
    5. Lattice Isomorphism Theorem.
      Let \(R\) be a ring, let \(N\) be a R-submodule of \(M\text{,}\) and let \(\pi: M \to M/N\) be the canonical quotient map. Then the function
      \begin{equation*} \Psi : \{R-\text{submodules of } M \text{ containing }N\} \to \{R-\text{submodules of }M/N\} \end{equation*}
      defined by \(\Psi(K) = K/N\) is a bijection, with inverse given by \(\Psi^{-1}(T) = \pi^{-1}(T)\) for each \(R\) submodule \(T\) of \(M/N\text{.}\) Moreover, \(\Psi\) and \(\Psi^{-1}\) preserve sums and intersections.

    Proof.

    Ignoring the rules for scaling by \(R\text{,}\) we know each of the first four results holds for abelian groups (and the maps are the same). So, we merely need to prove that the rules for scaling are respected in each case. In more detail:
    For the UMP, we already know that \(\ov{f}\) is a well-defined homomorphism of groups under \(+\) and that it is the unique one such that \(\ov{f} \circ \can = f\text{.}\) It remains only to show \(\ov{f}\) preserves scaling: This follows quickly from the definitions:
    \begin{equation*} \ov{f}(r (m +N)) = \ov{f} (rm + N) = f(rm) = r f(m) = r \ov{f}(m + N). \end{equation*}
    where the third equation uses that \(f\) preserves scaling.
    For the First Isomorphism Theorem, we already know that there is an isomorphism of abelian groups under \(+\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \ov{h}: M/\ker(h) \xra{\cong} \im(h), \end{equation*}
    given by \(\ov{h}(m + \ker(h)) = h(m)\text{,}\) and it remains only to show this map preserves scaling. This is a special case of what we proved in part (0).
    For the second isomorphism theorem, we need to first check that \(A+B\) and \(A \cap B\) are submodules. From 817 we already know they are subgroups under \(+\text{,}\) and it is evident from the definitions that each is closed under scaling by elements of \(R\text{.}\) Now, we know from 817 that there is an isomorphism of abelian groups \(h: A/(A \cap B) \xra{\cong} (A+B)/B\) given by \(h(a + (A\cap B)) = a + B\text{.}\) It remains only to show \(h\) preserves scaling:
    \begin{equation*} h(r(a+(A \cap B))) = h(ra + A \cap B) = ra + B = r(a +B) = rh(a + (A \cap B)). \end{equation*}
    For the third, we already know (from 817) that \(B/A\) is a subgroup of \(M/A\) under \(+\text{.}\) Given \(r \in R\) and \(b +A \in B/A\) we have \(r(b+A) = rb + A\) which belongs to \(B/A\) since \(rb \in B\text{.}\) This proves \(B/A\) is a submodule of \(M/A\text{.}\) Also from 817 we know there is an isomorphism of abelian groups \(h: (M/A)/(B/A) \to M/B\) given by \(h((m+A) + B/A) = m + B\) and it remains only to show it is \(R\)-linear: \(h(r((m+A) + B/A)) =h(r(m+A) + B/A) = h((rm + A) + B/A) = rm + B = r(m +B) = r h((m+A) + B/A)\text{.}\)
    The Lattice Theorem is the most complicaed to gerenlize. From 817 we know thre is a bijection between the set of subgroups of \(M\) and that contain \(N\) and subgroups of the quotient group \(M/N\text{,}\) and the maps are the same as given in the statment. We just need to prove that these maps send submodules to submodules. If \(K\) is a submodule of \(M\) containing \(N\text{,}\) then by part (3) we know \(K/N\) is a submodule of \(M/N\text{.}\)
    If \(T\) is a submodule of \(M/N\text{,}\) then \(\pi^{-1}(T)\) is an abelian group. For \(r \in R\) adn \(m \in \pi^{-1}(T)\) we have \(\pi(m) \in T\) and hence \(\pi(rm) = r\pi(m) \in T\) too, since \(T\) is a submodule. This proves \(\pi^{-1}(T)\) is a submodule.
