Theorem 1.5. Properties of Groups.

Let \(G\) be a group.
  1. Unique Identity.
    The element \(e\in G\) satisfying Item 2 of Definition 1.2 is unique, and we thus refer to it as the identity element of \(G\text{.}\)
  2. Unique Inverses.
    For each \(x\in G\text{,}\) the element satisfying Item 3 of Definition 1.2 is unique, and we thus refer to it as the inverse of \(x\text{.}\)
  3. Cancellation.
    Suppose \(x,y,z\in G\text{.}\) If \(xy = xz\) then \(y=z\text{.}\) Similarly, if \(yx = zx\) we have \(y = z\)

Proof.

  1. Suppose there exist two elements \(e\) and \(e'\) such that \(ex = x = xe\) and \(e'x = x = xe'\) for all \(x\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} e = ee' = e'. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(e=e'\text{,}\) making the identity element unique.
  2. For a given \(x\text{,}\) if \(yx = xy = e\) and \(zx = xz = e\) for some \(y\) and \(z\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} z &= ez \\ &= (yx)z \\ &= y(xz) \\ &= ye \\ &= y. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Thus \(y=z\text{,}\) making inverses unique.
  3. Suppose \(xy = xz\) for some \(x,y,z\in G\text{.}\) By the Item 3, there exists some \(x\inv\in G\) such that \(xx\inv=e=x\inv x\text{.}\) Observe
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} xy &= xz \\ x\inv(xy) &= x\inv(xz) & \text{multiplying on the left by }x\inv \\ (x\inv x)y &= (x\inv x)z & \text{associativity} \\ (e)y &= (e)z & \text{inverses}\\ y &= z & \text{identity}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
