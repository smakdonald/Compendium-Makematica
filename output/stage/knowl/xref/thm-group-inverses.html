Theorem 1.15. Properties of Inverses.

If \(G\) is a group and \(x,y\in G\text{,}\) then
  1. \((x^{-1})^{-1} = x\) and
  2. \(\displaystyle (xy)\inv=y\inv x\inv\)

Proof.

  1. We show that both \(x\) and \((x^{-1})^{-1}\) are inverses of the element \(x\inv\text{.}\) First, recall from Definition 1.3 that \(x\cdot x\inv=e\text{.}\) However, it is also the case that \((x\inv)\inv\cdot x\inv=e\text{.}\) By part (2) of Theorem 1.5 we know that inverses are unique, and thus it must be the case that \((x^{-1})^{-1} = x\text{.}\)
  2. By definition, \((xy)\inv\) is the inverse of the element \(xy\text{.}\) However, we also have
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} (xy)(y\inv x\inv) &= x(yy\inv)x\inv \\ &= x(e)x\inv\\ &= (xe)x\inv \\ &= xx\inv \\ &= e. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Thus both \((xy)\inv\) and \(y\inv x\inv\) are inverses of \((xy)\text{,}\) making them equal by part (2) of Theorem 1.5.
